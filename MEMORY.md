## Key Themes

### Alcoholism — Progression Across Generations
- The book opens with Glenn's alcohol poisoning (Chapter 1) as a deliberate structural choice
- **Symbolic logic:** By the time alcoholism reaches Glenn, it has escalated through at least 4 generations — Shetland Islands grandfather → Noel → Gary → Glenn
- The Shetland father is the origin point: alcoholic, petty thief, migrant. Noel and Gordon were reacting *against* him when they built the empire — but they carried it anyway
- The opening is meant to show the *accumulated weight* of inherited addiction — each generation passed it down heavier
- Glenn wants to track the **progression and unmanageability** of alcoholism as a thread running through all three men across the whole book
- This theme should be developed further through the book — not just stated but shown in how each man's relationship with alcohol looked different but served the same function

### Gary's Alcoholism Arc
- Gary is a **top-up drinker** — maintenance mode, always slightly steadied, never visibly drunk, never fully sober
- His drinking is managed and hidden; the "careful drink to steady himself" version
- His defining arc moment: the **Jeremy scene** (c.1997–2000) — Gary is seen by Glenn's childhood friend Jeremy at a strip club/brothel owned by Jeremy's father. Gary is drunk, out of control, and asks Jeremy if he can get him some cocaine. Jeremy feels sick for Glenn — it's humiliating by proxy. They kick Gary out of the club.
- **The water fight (setup/contrast scene):** Jeremy is also the ONLY person Glenn ever saw Gary be genuinely playful with — during a water fight. Gary had lightness in him; he just never aimed it at his son. Water fight establishes Gary's capacity for warmth; the brothel scene shows where that man ended up.
- Arc shape: water fight (Gary playful, human, loose) → brothel scene (Gary degraded, asking Glenn's friend for coke, thrown out) = the full collapse of the managed man
- Also: Glenn's friend Chris is part of this friend group

### Violence as Inheritance
- Noel was violent — pub fights, domestic, built something in Freeman's Bay through force
- Glenn inherited the same blood — identified in the first-fight chapter (Glenfield primary school, age 6)
- "At six years old on a grass hill in Glenfield, I had no idea I was carrying him. I just got up."

### The grieving son
- Gary's complicated grief at Noel's deathbed — wanting something from a man who could never give it, watching that possibility close forever
- Glenn's contempt for Gary's grief at 20 — something he spent 26 years unlearning

## Characters

### Gordon Thompson
- Noel's **younger brother** — not a worker, co-builder of the empire alongside Noel
- Slightly less hardened than Noel, but still a hard man — menacing in his own right
- Key arc moment: at some point in the novel, Gordon expresses **distaste for Noel's abuse of his children and Barbara** — a line he won't cross. To be explored later.
- The brotherly bond between them is forged partly by shared origin: a **loser, alcoholic, petty thief father who migrated from the Shetland Islands**
- That father is the generation *before* Noel — the alcoholism thread goes back even further than previously noted
- Appears in The Warehouse chapter (Ch 4)

### The Shetland Islands Father (unnamed so far)
- Noel and Gordon's father — Thompson family ancestry is from the **Shetland Islands**
- Migration path: Shetland Islands → fishing trip to Australia → jumped to New Zealand
- **Chronic alcoholic**, constantly getting locked up, a terrible father
- Noel looked down on him as a loser — actively wanted to be nothing like him: strong, respected, not weak
- This is the engine of Noel's character: he built himself in *opposition* to his father
- The cruelest irony of the whole book: Noel despised weakness and alcoholism so much that he overcorrected into brutality — and still passed the drinking down, just repackaged
- The alcoholism thread: Shetland father → Noel → Gary → Glenn (4 generations minimum)
