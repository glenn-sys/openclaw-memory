Chapter One: Near Death


The carpet is in my mouth.

Synthetic. Cheap. The kind that holds cigarette smell from the decade before you moved in. It's pressed against my lips and cheek — the right side of my face — and I can feel every strand of it but I can't move away. There's a lamp at the edge of my vision. Its shade is at the wrong angle, tilted like it got bumped and no one straightened it. Orange light. Warm and still.

Something is wrong with me.

I know this the way you know things in the last moments before sleep, when your thoughts are already breaking apart — not knowledge exactly, more a fact arriving from a great distance, very slowly, already late.

I'm on the floor. I don't know how I got on the floor.

A door. Voices.

"Oh god—"

My mother. That's my mother's voice.

There was a party. There were boys from school and somebody's older brother who got the bottles and there was bourbon, sweet and burning, and I kept going because the going felt like power, like finally — and then —

Gap. Dark gap where several hours used to be.

"Glenn."

His hands are under my arms. My father's hands — I know them without looking, big and dry, the hands I watched do up buttons on my school shirt when I was small and couldn't manage them myself. He's lifting me. The carpet falls away from my face. I want to say something to him. I can't find where my voice went. He's talking but the sound keeps dropping out, like a radio station you're driving away from, and I hear *come on* and *stay with me* and his voice has a quality I haven't heard from him before — scraped raw, frightened — and that frightens me more than anything else has.

Cold air.

Auckland night air. Damp. The smell of cut grass somewhere. The sound of an engine running. I'm being folded into a back seat, held on my side, and my father is very close, his mouth near my ear, saying something I can't hold onto, the words keep breaking up, and his hand is flat against my sternum.

I can feel my own heart under his palm. I wish I hadn't noticed that.

Someone is driving very fast. Through the gap between the front seats I can see streetlights going past in strips — amber, dark, amber, dark — and there's a red light, I register that we don't stop, and the car takes a corner hard and my father holds me tighter. Rob. That's Rob driving. I can tell from the set of his shoulders, forward and tense, both hands on the wheel like the wheel is the only thing keeping everything from flying apart. Rob who came to every barbecue. Rob who is now running lights without slowing.

My father says: "Stay with me."

Quiet. Certain. Like a command he has authority to give.

I try.

The nausea comes without warning and my father rolls me and holds my hair back and there is nothing dignified about any of it — I am fifteen years old and I am vomiting in the back seat of Rob's car and my father is holding me and saying something but the sounds keep dissolving before they become words. After, he wipes my mouth with his sleeve. I know that's what he does. I feel the fabric.

His hand goes back to my chest.

Dark.

Cold linoleum under my back. Lights above me, too bright, the particular fluorescent white of a place that doesn't close. People moving quickly — not running, more deliberate than that, purposeful. Voices with the shape of urgency:

*What did he take—*

*—how long ago—*

*—breathing, he's breathing—*

They're talking about me in the third person, as if I arrived on the table rather than being a person who walked through this morning in a school uniform and ate cereal and got on with being fifteen. I want to correct this. I can't.

My father's face. He's on the other side of something — glass, a window — and he's very still. Standing the way men stand when they know there's nothing to do with their hands. He's watching me the way I will later, much later, watch other people through glass, unable to reach them, unable to fix what I broke.

I don't know what expression he's wearing. I can't read it from here, from this side of whatever is happening to me. I won't understand that expression until I'm forty-six and standing in a similar doorway myself.

A sound.

The machine.

The machine starts making a sound — fast, panicked, rising — and people are moving toward me and hands are on my chest and I am very tired, more tired than I have ever been, the kind of tired that has nothing to do with sleep. It's a letting go kind of tired. Everything heavy and soft and receding. The light from overhead is so white it's almost —

Nothing.

Black.

Nothing.


—


I'm forty-six years old.

My daughter is leaving for university.

I have to stop when I write that. Let it sit there. Dana. Eighteen years old, and I keep thinking about how when I was fifteen I was on that carpet. His hands on my chest in the back seat, willing my heart to stay in time. And now she's going. Out into a world I have no control over, carrying blood I haven't fully explained to her yet.

This is not a story I wanted to write. But Dana is leaving, and I know things about this family that I cannot protect her from just by keeping quiet. I know how the blood runs. I know how damage passes down without meaning to — not just from fathers to sons but sideways, underneath, into the women who love these men and the daughters who grow up watching. The way a father teaches a child who to look for, what feels like safety, what feels like home — without ever meaning to teach any of it.

I nearly died on a Friday night in 1994. My parents came home from a party and found me on the living room floor. My father — Gary — scooped me up and got me to the car and Rob drove us to Takapuna A&E and my heart went into arrhythmia in the emergency room while Gary stood at the window and watched.

I was fifteen. I was showing off. That's the whole story, and it isn't.

What I've just given you are the fragments — because fragments are all I have. The carpet in my mouth. The cold air. The stripped amber of streetlights. My father's voice scraping against the dark. It doesn't come back to me in sequence, doesn't arrive with the clean causality of a story that knows what it is. It arrives like it happened: badly, in pieces, with huge amounts missing.

What I remember most clearly, when I remember anything at all, are his hands.

The warmth of them. That flat, steady pressure on my chest in the back seat, as if he could personally hold my heart in rhythm just by pressing hard enough. Gary Thompson, who had done a great many things I would spend years being angry at. Gary Thompson, who was in the process of becoming someone complicated and disappointing and human in all the ways I wasn't old enough yet to understand. That night, in the back of Rob's car, on the way to a hospital neither of us wanted to need, he held me together with his hands.

I didn't know then that was a kind of love. I didn't know that love and damage could live in the same pair of hands, could come from the same man, could be the realest thing about him.

I know now.

That's the thing about writing this. You start with what you remember — the carpet, the cold, the orange light from the tilted lamp — and what you find underneath is the story you didn't know you were telling.

My name is Glenn Thompson. I am forty-six years old. I have four rehabs behind me and one relapse in front of me that I'm still too close to write about clearly. I have a daughter, Dana, who loves me and keeps her distance, which is reasonable given the evidence. She is eighteen and leaving for university and she doesn't know what she's carrying.

I'm writing this down so he knows where he comes from. All of it. Because the alternative — silence, the careful silence this family has always mistaken for dignity — is the thing that nearly killed me long before the drinking did.

This is a story about three men.

My grandfather Noel, who confused power with worth and spent his whole life making everyone around him pay for the confusion.

My father Gary, who knew what Noel had done and tried to become something different and mostly didn't.

And me. Still here. Still working on it. Writing this in the hope that understanding a wound is the first step toward not passing it on.

We'll see.
