Chapter Two: Bloodlines


Let me tell you about two kitchens.

The first kitchen belongs to a man named Bill Fairweather — my mother Heather's grandfather — and it smells of butter and cigarette smoke and the particular sweetness of a farm house in summer, when all the windows are open and the wind comes off the Waikato paddocks carrying grass seed and manure and something clean underneath all of it. Bill is standing at the bench mixing something in a tin jug, unhurried, pleased with the task. His hands are broad and patient — a man who has spent forty years coaxing things from earth. He turns and he's smiling and the kitchen is full of cousins, everyone underfoot, and he says to the room — to whoever will take it — *here, try this*, and he pours a little of whatever's in the jug into a teacup and passes it around the children like it's communion.

It was communion, of sorts. Warm beer and ginger ale, maybe. Or scrumpy, if the season was right. My mother told me this. She told me how the grown-up taste of it made her feel chosen. How Bill Fairweather died when she was nineteen — heart attack, sixty years old, used up — and how his kitchen stayed warm in her memory in a way that most places don't.

The second kitchen belongs to a man named Noel Thompson, which is to say it belongs to my grandfather, which is to say it was my father's kitchen when he was small.

This kitchen I can only construct from wreckage. From the things my aunts told me. From my father's face when he said certain words, or didn't say them. From the silences that were always, in that family, shaped like something that happened before I arrived.

What I know is this: it was not warm.

---

They lost two children before my father Gary was old enough to understand what death was.

Bruce first, tetanus. He was three. Then Raewyn, lupus — she made it to seven, which is long enough to leave a shape in the house, long enough for the other children to understand she was gone rather than just never there. My grandmother Barbara kept their photographs on a shelf in the hall, and nobody touched that shelf, not ever. That was the rule. Not a spoken rule — Noel's rules were rarely spoken. They were established through one incident that everyone understood and after that they were simply the law.

Grief is strange in a house like that. It doesn't get to be grief. It turns into something else — into distance, into silence, into the specific quality of attention Noel paid to the living children that told them they could go the same way and he would cope. That's not what he meant. I don't think that's what he meant. But it's what landed.

---

The night that my Aunt Noela talks about happened on a Friday. She was nine. Gary was seven, which makes this 1961, and they had been in bed for two hours when the lounge room started getting loud.

You need to understand what kind of house this was in what kind of street. Freeman's Bay. 1961. Ponsonby Road a block over and if you had money you lived on Ponsonby Road and if you didn't you lived on the streets behind it, the tilted little streets where the houses backed up against each other and everyone knew everyone's noise. Noel had been drinking since teatime — they all knew the geography of it, the slow deterioration of the silences between rooms that told you where you were in the night.

When the door opened, the light from the hall was enough.

Noela came out of sleep the way you do when you're nine and something has changed in the air. Gary was already awake — she told me that. He was sitting up in bed, watching the door, and he had the particular stillness of a child who has learned that stillness is protective.

Noel filled the doorway. He was a big man. He is a big man in every story I have ever been told — not tall, exactly, but dense with something, an accumulated weight that had nothing to do with his body. He had the smell about him of the pub and before that the warehouse, the sacking and raw wool dust that never quite washed out. He looked at his children in their beds and he said:

"Up."

That was all.

They got up. Gary fumbling with his blanket, Noela standing barefoot on the lino — the cold of it coming straight up through her feet. They followed him to the lounge room where there were men sitting in the good chairs and on the sofa, men my grandfather had brought back from somewhere, big men with big hands and the blurred quality of people who've been drinking since afternoon. One of them had a bottle. The wireless was going — some race call, the crackling voice of the caller filling the room with the names of horses.

"Right," Noel said. "My kids."

He said it the way a man introduces a party trick. The men looked up. One of them grinned.

"The girl sings," Noel said. "And this one" — his hand dropping onto Gary's shoulder, the weight of it — "this one can tell you the last six All Black test squads."

Gary could do that. He'd learned it the way children learn things they're never explicitly taught — by understanding what was required, by hearing the pleasure in his father's voice when he got it right, by understanding what happened in the absence of pleasure. He stood in his pyjamas in front of these strangers and he recited the names. Meads, Whineray, Clarke — his voice was steady. Seven years old and his voice was steady in front of men three times his size, which is not a normal thing for a seven-year-old to be, and the fact that it was ordinary to him should tell you something about what was ordinary in that house.

They clapped. One of them gave Gary a shilling.

Noel stood at the edge of the room watching his children perform, and on his face — according to Noela, who was watching him because she had learned that watching him was the safest thing — was an expression that might from a distance have looked like pride.

Later. Much later. After the men had gone and the bottle was empty and something in the night had shifted.

I don't know what happened after that. Noela stops at a certain point in the telling and I have learned not to ask her to continue. Some things have a shape you can feel in the silence around them, and you don't require the details to understand the weight.

---

The morning after was the toy run.

I've heard about this from three different people in the family and they all tell it slightly differently and the differences are themselves the point.

Noela says Noel came into the kitchen at nine o'clock with two paper bags from Woolworths and put them on the table and said nothing, and in the bags were a doll for her and a cricket set for Gary. The cricket set had a real bat — not a toy bat, a proper bat, a child's bat but real — and Gary turned it over in his hands and looked at his father and said thank you.

My father Gary never talked about the toys directly. What he said, once, when we were both drinking and the night had gotten late and loose, was that his father had a way of making things even. That was the phrase — *making things even*. He said it the way you say a thing when the word you mean and the word you can reach aren't the same word.

What I think is this: a man like Noel Thompson couldn't apologise. Apology requires a kind of accounting — *I did this, it was wrong, I am sorry* — and that accounting would undo every assumption he had about himself. So instead there were toys. There was cricket equipment and dolls from Woolworths, placed on a kitchen table without comment, and if you wanted to receive them you received them, and if you wanted to say something about the night before you said it into the silence and the silence ate it.

Gary received the bat.

What else could he do.

---

My mother Heather grew up on a dairy farm outside Morrinsville. Bill Fairweather's farm — a hundred and fifty acres, Friesians, the kind of life that is hard in different ways than Freeman's Bay is hard. Physical. Seasonal. People being decent to each other because decency is what you've got when the weather turns and your neighbours are two kilometres away.

The first time Gary went to that farm he was twenty-three. He and Heather had been together a year and he had never been anywhere that quiet.

I'm speculating here, because neither of them told me this story the way I needed them to. But I know what the farm is like — I've been there — and I know what Gary was like, and I know what a man who grew up in Noel Thompson's house would feel when he walked into a place where people sat down to dinner together without scanning the room first.

They ate at six. Bill Fairweather sat at the head of the table and asked his grandchildren questions he actually listened to the answers of. His wife Shirley passed things without being asked. The conversation went back and forth across the table the way conversation does when nobody in it is performing, when nobody is calculating the angles, when the room isn't pressurised with the proximity of a man who needs the people in it to be small.

Gary would have been quiet that first time. He was always quiet in rooms where he didn't know the rules, and the rule in that room — that people could simply speak without it meaning something dangerous — was so foreign to him that quiet was the only safe response.

Heather told me once that he cried in the car on the way home. She said she didn't ask him about it and he didn't explain and they drove back to Auckland in the dark. She said he held her hand the whole way, his thumb moving across her knuckles. That's all she said.

That's enough.

I think my father loved my mother with a ferocity that had nothing to do with her specifically — or not only her — but with what she represented. A door into a different kind of life. A family that knew how to be warm. He married her and he believed — he must have believed — that he was crossing into something new, that Noel Thompson's house was behind him and the Fairweather kitchen was ahead, that a man could choose.

He could choose. He just couldn't choose as far as he thought he could.

---

Here is the thing they don't tell you about poison: it doesn't announce itself. It doesn't arrive with a label. It gets in early and it changes the chemistry of things gradually, over years, until the changed state seems like the normal state and you have lost your reference point.

Noel Thompson was a brutal man. His children knew it and carried it. Gary Thompson tried, and I mean this seriously — he genuinely tried. He put distance between himself and Freeman's Bay. He married warmth. He wanted to do better. And he mostly did — mostly — except when he drank, and when he drank the distance collapsed, and in the absence of distance you find out what's in the blood.

He didn't beat us. I want to be clear about that, because it matters, because it's the line he drew and he held it. He drew the line one place further out than his father did. That's not nothing. That's also not enough.

What he gave us instead: the long cold silences when he was elsewhere in his head. The smell of him when he came home wrong. The particular quality of my mother's stillness when he came through the door — not fear, but the thing that lives next door to fear, the adjustment, the calibration. His absences — physical, then emotional — opening up around us the way water opens up when you pull something out of it: no evidence it was ever full.

I thought for a long time that I was different.

I was not different.

But I'm getting ahead of myself.

The point I'm making is this: the pattern was old before I was born. It lived in the walls of a house in Freeman's Bay before it lived in ours. And when I was fifteen years old and lying on the living room carpet with my lips turning blue and the smell of the carpet in my mouth, it was having its way with me too — I just didn't know yet what to call it.

My daughter is eighteen. She leaves for university in the autumn.

Now you understand why I'm writing this down.
