Chapter Four: The Warehouse


The sack was on the floor and Noel was crouched over it.

Jute. It had the smell of all jute — lanolin and seawater and the long dark of a ship's hold — but this one had something else under that. Sharper. He pressed two fingers against the weave and leaned close. Faintly chemical. You wouldn't catch it unless you knew what you were after.

He stood up and rolled his right hand into a fist. The knuckles had gone stiff again. The sailor from Thursday night, whose name Noel hadn't caught and hadn't needed, had a harder jaw than he'd looked to have. No lasting damage, but the hand reminded him in cold air. He flexed it once, set it at his side.

Across the warehouse floor, Gordon was lifting another sack from the bale with both hands, grunting with the weight of it.

"This the lot?" Noel said.

"This is the lot." Gordon set the sack down and straightened. He was a small-boned man made unreliable-looking by a face that arranged itself naturally into apology, though he wasn't sorry about much. He'd been working with Noel for three years. Long enough to know when to push and when to wait.

"Forty-two bales," Gordon said. "Same as last time."

Noel walked the length of the floor. Late afternoon, the light coming in through the high windows at the north end — four of them, three intact and one with a crack running corner to corner held together with a strip of tape gone brown with age. Below the windows, the wall was marked with water stain in the rough shape of a map of somewhere imaginary. He'd looked at that stain a hundred times and never decided what it was a map of.

Outside: Freeman's Bay doing what it always did. A cart horse on the road. Someone's radio through a window — a woman's voice rising and then cut off. The gulls over the harbour. He'd grown up three streets over from here, in a house that had fewer rooms than this warehouse had windows, and the sounds hadn't changed in thirty years.

His brother Andrew used to say Freeman's Bay taught you to count.

*Count your luck*, Andrew meant. That you were here rather than somewhere worse, that you'd eaten, that nobody had put you in the ground. Noel had thought that was a pathetic accounting at the time and had said so. Andrew had laughed, which was what Andrew did when he thought Noel was missing the point.

That was 1943. Andrew went to the Pacific and Noel went to the tanneries and one of them came back counting different things.

He turned away from the windows.

"Forty-two's not enough," he said.

Gordon pulled a face. "Forty-two's what we've got."

"I know what we've got. I'm telling you what we need." He kicked the sack on the floor gently, a testing kick, the kind a man gives a tyre. "Hartley's are running forty a week through their place on College Hill. We move forty-two in a month, we're nobody."

"We're starting up." Gordon spread his hands. "You've got to start somewhere."

"Aye." Noel looked at him. "But where you start is where people put you."

Gordon was quiet for a moment. Then: "So we go back to Carmichael."

"Carmichael wants more than I'm giving him."

"What does he want?"

"He wants in."

The word landed and sat there. Gordon looked at the bales stacked against the far wall. He seemed to be doing arithmetic in his head that had nothing to do with numbers.

"In," Gordon said.

"That's what he said."

"What did you say?"

"I said I'd think about it."

The warehouse had the quality of a place that had absorbed many conversations over many years — the corrugated iron walls gave nothing back, deadened sound, made every exchange feel private by default. There were men in Freeman's Bay who had done business in rooms like this their whole lives. They understood that certain conversations required the particular silence of a place where no one came unless invited.

"There's other suppliers," Gordon said, carefully.

"Two others. Henderson and McLay. Henderson's drinking himself sideways, won't last the year. McLay's an old woman about his stock, won't move anything he can't account for in a ledger." Noel's jaw moved. "Carmichael's the one with the import line. We need the volume."

"So we give him what he wants."

Noel crouched down again over the sack on the floor. Ran his thumb along the seam. The stitching was uneven at the bottom, hurried or amateur — someone had been in a rush to close this one. He pinched the corner of the fabric between finger and thumb and pulled. Two stitches parted. He put the opened seam under his nose and breathed.

Definitely chemical. Not wool processing. Something else.

He set it back down carefully, the same way he found it.

"Not yet," he said.

"What are you waiting for?"

Noel stood. The stiff hand he kept at his side. Through the cracked window, the gulls were lifting off something in the road, spiralling up on the updraft from the harbour, white against the white of the late afternoon sky. When he was young he used to try to count them. He'd never managed it — they moved too fast, collapsed and regrouped — and Andrew had laughed at him for that too.

"There's stuff going through here," Noel said, "that isn't ours to know about. Yet." He looked at the sack on the floor. "Carmichael's been moving it for two years. Same trucks as the wool. Same manifests, same drivers." He looked up. "He doesn't advertise it. But he doesn't hide it either, not from the right people."

Gordon's face had gone careful. "What kind of stuff?"

Noel looked at him for a long moment.

"Stuff that needs special handling."

He said it the way a man says the last line of a story — not the end of the story, but the end of what he's prepared to say. He picked up the sack and dropped it on the bale with the others and began doing up his coat.

"Get the doors," he said.

Gordon got the doors.

Outside, the cart horse was gone. The street was gravel and mud in equal measure, the late light making long shadows of the fence posts running down to the water. Noel pulled his collar up and lit a cigarette from the tin in his breast pocket. The match flared and he cupped it against the harbour wind, and for a moment his face was lit from below — jaw, cheekbones, the particular set of his eyes — and then the flame went out and the dark came back.

Andrew had said: *count your luck*.

He was. He was counting it right now, and it looked good.

He walked home.
