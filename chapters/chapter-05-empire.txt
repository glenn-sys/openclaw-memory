Chapter Five: Empire


The sacks came off the truck in a chain — Gordon at the tail, Noel at the warehouse door, each one passed hand to hand and stacked against the near wall with the others. Forty-seven sacks of wheat. Forty-eight. Forty-nine.

Gordon counted aloud. He always did. Noel didn't correct him.

The morning had come in grey off the harbour and stayed that way. Through the high window above the stack the sky was the colour of a wet road. Noel could hear the gulls working something over near the water. He didn't look up.

He had taken the warehouse on a three-year lease in February. The rent was reasonable because the building had been used for cold storage and had the smell of it — the ammonia and the dead cold — but that was going now, replaced by jute and grain and the lanolin smell of the wool clip when it came through. The ledger showed seven regular clients. The sack trucks were his own, bought secondhand from a carrier in Newmarket who'd gone under in the spring. His name was on the side of them in new white paint.

*N. Thompson. Cartage.*

Gordon set the last sack down and straightened his back.

"Fifty-two," he said.

"Fifty-two," Noel said.

They'd been short. Noel made a note in the ledger and set it on the desk in the corner. He'd take it up with the supplier in the morning, or he wouldn't. The shortage was four sacks. He would decide what it was worth.

Eddie Moran came in at half ten. He didn't knock because there was no door to knock on — the warehouse opened at the front with a roll-shutter that Noel kept up during business hours — and he walked to the centre of the floor and stood there with his hat in his hands, turning it by the brim.

Gordon was out the back.

"Noel," Eddie said.

"Eddie."

Eddie had a face that looked like it had been pressed. Flat nose. Flat cheeks. He'd come up from Dunedin two years ago and he was in timber or said he was, and he was in other things that he didn't say. Noel had drunk with him at Hogan's and he'd sat across a card table from him four or five times. He was not a man who lost when losing could be avoided.

"I've got a question," Eddie said.

Noel waited.

"You're running the trucks over to the port. Tuesdays and Fridays."

"Among other days."

"I know." Eddie turned the hat. "You've got a good relationship with the stevedores. The foreman — McGregor?"

"I know McGregor."

"Right." Eddie set his hat on the corner of the desk. He looked at the window and then at the stacked sacks and then at Noel. "There are goods coming through on the *Northland Star*. Three sailings — March, July, October. Coming in on pallets and they'd need to go onto your trucks. Go through the normal handling."

"What goods," Noel said.

"The manifest says agricultural equipment."

Noel looked at him.

"Right," Eddie said.

They stood in the grey light of the warehouse. Somewhere outside a truck was reversing, its engine complaining on a low note. The gulls again.

"The arrangement," Noel said. "You'd deal with McGregor directly."

"I'd deal with you. You'd handle McGregor."

"And the margin."

Eddie told him. He said it once. He didn't embellish it or apologise for it and Noel understood that the number was not the opening position.

Noel picked up his ledger and put it in the drawer.

"March," he said.

"Twenty-third."

"All right."

Eddie picked up his hat. "I'll be in touch before then." He walked back to the shutter. He didn't extend his hand and Noel didn't move toward him. There was nothing to shake on. It was simply what was going to happen.

—

He came home at six. The door was unlocked — Barbara never locked it — and the house smelled of the stew she'd been making since the afternoon. He hung his jacket on the hook by the door and washed his hands at the kitchen sink and Barbara came in from the hall with one hand at the small of her back.

She was very large now. The child had dropped and she moved carefully. Her face had kept its shape but everything else had changed its geometry, and she moved through the kitchen like a ship adjusting to its load — deliberate, measured, not complaining.

"There's bread," she said.

"Right."

She set a bowl in front of him. He ate. She stood at the counter and ate hers standing, which she'd started doing in the last month because sitting had become difficult. She didn't ask about his day.

There was a sound from the hall. Small feet on bare boards.

The boy came to the kitchen doorway and stood there. Four years old. He had his mother's colouring, dark, and the look of a child who has learned to assess a room before entering it. He stood at the edge of the linoleum with his hands at his sides.

Noel ate.

Barbara set down her bowl. "Lou. Come and have your supper."

The boy came in and climbed onto the chair at the far end of the table. Barbara put a small bowl in front of him and he ate with a spoon, carefully, not looking anywhere in particular.

Noel finished his stew. He cut a piece of bread and used it on the bowl and ate that too. He pulled the bread toward him and cut a second piece. He was aware of the child at the far end of the table the way he was aware of the chair he was sitting on — something present, structural, not requiring thought.

Barbara asked him about a bill that had come. He told her to put it on the desk. She nodded.

Later, when the boy had been put to bed and Barbara was moving slowly through the house doing the last things she did before sleep, Noel sat at the kitchen table with the ledger open and went through the columns. The sack hire. The cartage rates. The fuel.

In March there would be a new line. It wouldn't appear in the ledger as anything other than what it needed to appear as.

He did the figures in his head. Then again on paper. The number was different from what he'd been earning and the difference was large.

His father had died in 1939 with eleven pounds in the post office and a reputation for honesty that hadn't bought him a single thing in sixty-one years of living. Noel had stood at the graveside in the rain and felt nothing in particular except the cold and the finality of it, and he had decided something in that moment — or he had confirmed something that had already been decided, some arrangement he had made with himself in early boyhood — and the decision was simply this: that a man's measure was what he had built.

Not what he was owed. Not what he deserved. What he had built.

The child in Barbara's belly would come in six weeks, give or take. He had already decided it would be a boy. The decision had no practical force — he understood that — but it was there regardless. He would have a son. The son would come into something. He would look at what his father had made and it would be real, measurable, a thing that had weight and could be held.

He closed the ledger and set it in the drawer and turned off the kitchen light.

Through the bedroom wall he could hear Barbara's breathing — slow, effortful, the breathing of a body with too much to carry. He did not go in.

He sat in the dark kitchen for a while longer. The street outside was quiet. The harbour was somewhere below the houses, invisible, making its sounds.

He had no feeling he could name about what was going to happen. He had agreed to a thing and the thing would occur and there would be money from it and he would put the money to use. That was the full extent of it.

He was thirty-one years old. He was just getting started.
