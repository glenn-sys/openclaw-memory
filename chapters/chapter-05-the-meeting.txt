Chapter Five: The Meeting


The Harbour Arms on a Friday.

Smoke so thick you tasted it before you pushed through the door. Beer and cigarettes and the sweet-rot of the carpet — twenty years of spilled drinks ground into the pattern until the pattern was gone and what remained was its own thing, dark and permanent. The noise of it: twenty separate conversations and the wireless above the bar and someone's laugh that cut through everything else — a woman's laugh, sharp, deployed like a warning. Low amber light. The ceiling pressing down.

Barbara Winslow had been coming here for two years and it still did something to her stomach when she walked in. Not fear. Something that lived next to fear in the way the Harbour Arms lived next to the streets of Freeman's Bay — close enough to touch.

She got her beer and found a space at the end of the bar.

She was twenty-four. She worked at the biscuit factory on Wellington Street — early mornings, the ache in her feet by noon, the particular smell of warm sugar that clung to her hair and her clothes and that she could not, no matter how many times she washed, fully get out. She had come to Auckland from Hawke's Bay three years ago with fifteen pounds and one introduction, which was to a cousin who shared a two-room flat in Newton and made it clear the sharing arrangement was temporary. She was still in Newton. The cousin had long since moved on.

She had nobody to talk to about this.

That was the clean version of the situation. The full version was longer and considerably less clean and the one thing she refused to do was sit in her flat and go over it in her head again. The Harbour Arms was noise and movement and other people's problems, and right now that was exactly what she needed.

Jimmy was here. She saw him before he saw her — that was always better with Jimmy. He was at the far corner of the bar with two men she didn't know, his back mostly toward her, and from this angle he looked fine. He always looked fine from a distance. It was proximity that showed you the calibration: how his eyes moved before the rest of him moved, the way he held his glass too hard.

She should leave.

She knew she should leave. She had known it for the better part of a year, in the way you know things that are true but inconvenient — at a distance, with a kind of helpless academic clarity, while your actual life went on doing what it was doing. Jimmy had been a mistake from the second month. He had a wife in Devonport and a temper that ran ahead of his thinking and that particular quality of possessiveness that comes not from love but from the specific insecurity of a man who knows he hasn't earned the thing he's claiming.

He had put his hand around her wrist once and held it, in a doorway, while he told her calmly about certain consequences. She had been very still. After, she'd sat in the bathroom at the factory for twenty minutes.

She drank her beer.

He was turning. She saw the moment he found her — a small tightening, a recalibration, the smile that arrived half a beat after the eyes. He peeled away from his companions and came toward her through the crowd and she watched him come with a feeling she still didn't have the right name for. Dread, partly. But underneath the dread something that wanted the contact anyway, because the alternative was going home to the flat in Newton where there was nobody.

"Didn't know you'd be in," he said.

"Felt like a drink."

He put his hand on her back. High, the shoulder blade. Claiming. She felt the people around them notice it without looking up.

"You look good," he said, which was what he said when he wanted to establish possession without being obvious about it.

"Thanks."

They stood like that for a moment. Jimmy's two companions watched from across the bar without watching — the practiced peripheral attention of men who knew something might happen and weren't sure which way they wanted it to go. Barbara felt the room the way you feel weather before it breaks: the air changing, becoming specific.

She said: "I'm going home soon."

His hand on her back shifted. The pressure changed. Not harder — something more controlled than that. A demonstration of control, which is worse than hardness because it's deliberate.

"I don't think so," he said, pleasantly.

She moved to step back and his hand came around — not her wrist this time but her arm, above the elbow, and the grip was enough to hold her in place and no more, and the precision of it was its own kind of message. He was looking at her with the smile still on and his eyes doing the other thing.

What happened next was very fast.

A hand on Jimmy's collar. Not grabbing — just settling there, the weight of it, a hand the size of a small shovel. And Jimmy, who had been facing Barbara, was now somehow facing the other direction, and between one moment and the next the geometry of the situation had changed completely.

The man holding Jimmy's collar was no one she'd seen before. Medium height, built across the chest and shoulders with the density of someone who has done physical work his whole life. A face that was not handsome and not ugly — the kind of face you remember as having been there, as having been certain. Dark hair, a little long. His expression was completely equable.

He said, into Jimmy's ear: "That's not your hand to put anywhere."

Jimmy made a sound.

What happened after was ugly and brief. The man moved without winding up, without the performance of a fight beginning — it was simply already happening, and Jimmy was on the floor between two bar stools and the bar had gone the kind of quiet that will fill back up in sixty seconds because Freeman's Bay does not make a production of these things. People stepped back and then stepped back to where they'd been. The two companions found somewhere else to be.

The man — Noel, she would learn his name later that night — straightened his jacket with two pulls. Looked down at Jimmy, who was deciding whether to get up. Jimmy decided not to.

Then Noel looked at her.

Later, she would try to explain this to herself and fail. She would fail for years. She would look back and understand what she should have done — walked out, gone home to Newton, let the night be over — and she would not be able to explain why she didn't.

What she felt, looking at him, was not gratitude exactly. Or not only. The honest accounting of it: relief, yes. But relief could explain a lot of things without explaining all of them. What she felt had more to it — the particular lightening that comes when something frightening has been replaced by something more frightening on your behalf. The terrible comfort of a man who dispenses with problems the way you dispatch a wasp. You know what he is. You feel it in your blood, the cold animal recognition. And your blood says: *next to him you are safe*, and your brain says: *next to him you are not safe at all*, and the thing that decides is older than either of them.

She understood that the woman he went home with would never have to worry about men like Jimmy.

She did not understand yet what she would have to worry about instead. That is the tragedy of it: not stupidity. Not even blindness. Just the limited menu of available options for a woman alone in Freeman's Bay in 1947, and the thing that looked, from where she was standing, most like safety.

He didn't speak to her again that night. Moved back to the bar, took his glass, stood with his back to the room the way men stand when they own the room they're standing in. Eventually he glanced over — just that, a glance — and gave her the smallest nod. Not acknowledgement. More like registration. Like a man noting something he'll attend to later.

Then he walked out. The door swung closed behind him.

Barbara stood at the bar with her empty glass and the noise of the pub rising back around her, filling in the shape he'd left. Jimmy was gone — collected himself off the floor at some point and left, she hadn't seen when. The woman with the cutting laugh was laughing again from somewhere. Everything as it was before, plus one man's absence, minus something she could feel but not yet name.

She was going to marry him.

She didn't know that yet. But her body knew it before her mind did, in the way bodies do — something settling, some long-held tension shifting and releasing. The decision made without the decider's consent.

She looked at the door for a long time.

Then she went home.
