Chapter Seven: Performance Night


He heard the car before it turned into the drive.

He'd been lying in the dark for an hour already, maybe more — there was no clock in his room and he'd learned not to ask for one — listening to the street. He knew the Jaguar's engine the way he knew certain things: not by choice, but because knowing them had become necessary. The particular idle as his father eased off the accelerator. The way it ran on two seconds after the ignition cut.

He lay still.

Voices through the front door. His father's, and two others. The sound of glasses. The lounge light coming on — Gary could see it as a thin line under his door — and then laughter, his father's laugh, the one that didn't mean what laughter usually meant.

His door opened.

Noel was in his good suit. He smelled of cigarettes and whisky and the close warmth of a pub back room, and he filled the doorway the way he filled all doorways, with the density of someone who has decided he owns the space he occupies. Gary lay with his eyes already adjusted to the dark. His body had gone still before his mind caught up with it.

"Up," his father said.

Gary was already sitting up.

"Noela and Neil. Two minutes."

He went. He found his slippers without turning on the light. He went to Noela's room first — she was already awake, already at her door, already knowing — and then to Neil's, where he shook his brother by the shoulder until Neil surfaced, confused, his face crumpled with sleep.

"Come on," Gary said quietly. "Come on, mate."

—

There were two men in the lounge. One was Reg Faull, who Gary had seen before — a wide man, built low, with the look of someone who had never once in his life been on the losing side of a disagreement. The other he didn't know. They were in the good chairs with their glasses and their cigarettes and they looked at the three children in their pyjamas the way men look at things produced for their entertainment.

His father stood at the edge of the room. He had a glass and he was watching.

Barbara came in from the kitchen. She was in her dressing gown and her hair was still up and her face was doing the thing it did — bright, arranged, the smile that meant she was working very hard at something the men in the chairs would never notice.

"Oh, let them sit down — here, Reg, have something—" She had a plate. Crackers, cheese, done quickly. She extended it toward Reg Faull and kept talking, the brightness of her voice filling the room, buying time, buying what she could.

"The boy," Noel said.

The room went quiet.

Gary stepped forward. He felt his body arrange itself the way it always did — spine straight, weight balanced, face composed. He had been twelve years old for three months. He had been doing this for longer than he could remember.

"The All Black test squads," his father said. "Last six."

Gary said them. He'd memorised them the same way he'd memorised his father's moods — not because anyone had asked him to, but because being ready was the only protection available. He went through the names and the series and the coaches, steady and even, Meads and Tremain and White, all of it in sequence, and when he finished Reg Faull raised his eyebrows and the other man made a sound of approval and both of them clapped.

"Good lad." Reg put his hand in his pocket. A shilling appeared.

Gary took it. He said thank you.

Noela sang. She had always had a clear voice and she knew exactly what to do with it — she stood in the middle of the room and sang "Now Is the Hour" with her hands at her sides and her chin up, and the men were quiet, and Gary watched his father's face across the room and understood, as he had always understood, that whatever was on his face was not pride. It was the expression of a man presenting something he owns.

Neil stood where Gary had placed him and understood nothing except that he was very tired and that he should not cry.

Noela finished. The men clapped. Noel refilled their glasses. The night settled back into itself, the men talking among themselves, the children standing there in the way that children stand when they are waiting to be dismissed.

It was Reg Faull who asked to hear the piano.

Gary didn't know who had told Reg Faull about the piano. He hadn't known until that moment that anyone had. He felt his father look at him.

"He plays," his father said.

—

The piano was in the hall — an upright, dark wood, which had come with the house and which Gary had been having lessons on for eight months. His teacher was Mrs. Alderton on Ponsonby Road, Thursdays at four, and he was not bad. He was not good. He was somewhere in between, which under normal circumstances was fine, because normal circumstances meant Thursday afternoons and a woman with patient hands who said things like *from the top* and *watch the timing there*.

These were not normal circumstances.

He sat on the bench and the men stood in the hall doorway with their glasses and he put his hands on the keys. He could feel his father behind him without looking. He could feel the particular quality of his father's attention like pressure between his shoulder blades.

He played the opening of "Hey Jude." It had been everywhere that year — the radio, the pub, everyone humming it. Gary had learned it by ear, mostly. He could play the verse, the pre-chorus, most of the bridge. He knew where it got difficult.

The verse was fine.

The pre-chorus was fine.

The bridge came. His left hand went wrong — he felt it before it happened, felt the hesitation, the fraction of a second where his body and the music came apart — and then his fingers found the wrong notes and the sound that came out of the piano was nothing like music. A discord. A mistake, flat and final, hanging in the air of the hall.

Silence.

His father's hand came down on his shoulder. The grip of it.

"Again."

Gary started from the beginning. His hands were wet with sweat. He could feel each key separately now, feel the slight resistance of the action, feel the whole instrument as an obstacle. He played the verse. He played the pre-chorus. The bridge came again.

Same place.

His hands stopped completely. Ten fingers over the keys, useless, as if the signal between his brain and his hands had been cut.

The blow came from behind — open palm, behind the ear — and Gary went sideways off the bench. His elbow hit the side of the piano on the way down and a sharp white pain went up his arm and he was on the floor with the hallway floorboards against his cheek and the room tilting.

"Get up."

He got up. His ear was ringing. He put his hand on the piano bench to stand and Noel grabbed him by the hair — not the collar, the hair, a fistful of it above his ear — and hauled him upright, and Gary's eyes watered from the roots of it, from the specific pain of being lifted by something attached to your head, and he heard himself make a sound that he had not meant to make.

"Look at you." His father's breath was close, hot with whisky and something older than whisky, something that had been fermenting in him longer than tonight. "Look at you. Making me look like a fool in front of my friends."

He shook Gary by the hair once. The world went sideways.

Then Noela made a sound.

Not a cry. Just a sound — involuntary, small, the sound of a ten-year-old who has held herself together past the point where it's possible and something has slipped. And Barbara was already moving.

She came from the kitchen doorway. She walked past the men — past Reg Faull who had taken a step back and was studying the floor — and she put herself between Gary and his father and she said, very quietly: "Noel."

His father looked at her.

"Leave him," she said. "Leave them alone. They've done what you asked."

The silence was different now. The men in the hallway had the particular stillness of people who understand that something is going to happen and have decided that understanding it is the furthest they are willing to go.

What happened was fast.

Noel's fist caught her at the temple. Not a slap — a closed fist, full weight behind it, the weight of a big man who has been using his hands his entire adult life. The sound it made was not loud. Barbara went sideways into the piano. Her head connected with the corner of the upright — a different sound, worse, a sound Gary would not be able to describe or forget — and she went down. Her knees hit the floor and she caught herself on one hand and stayed there, her hair coming loose, blood starting immediately from her hairline, dark against the pale skin of her temple.

She stayed on her knees and she did not cry out.

"Mum," Noela said. One syllable. A child's voice.

Noel stepped back. He was breathing through his nose, even and slow, the way a man breathes when he has expended effort and is returning to his regular rhythm. He looked at the men in the doorway. He looked at his glass. He took a drink.

Barbara put one hand on the piano and pushed herself up. She stood. She pressed the back of her wrist against the cut at her temple and looked at her own blood and then she looked at her children.

"Bed," she said.

Her voice was steady.

Gary looked at his father's back. He looked at his mother's hand against her head. He looked at Reg Faull's shoes.

He took Neil by the wrist and he took Noela by the hand and he walked them up the hall to bed.

—

He lay in the dark and listened. The voices from the lounge went on for another hour. His father's laugh, a couple of times. The clink of glasses. Eventually the front door opened and closed twice and the car started in the drive and moved away up the street, and then the house was quiet.

He could hear his mother moving in the kitchen. Water running. The particular soft sounds of a woman alone after midnight, doing what needs to be done.

He didn't go out.

He lay on his back with his arm across his chest and the shilling in his closed fist, still warm, and he stared at the ceiling until the sounds from the kitchen stopped and the house was completely still.

—

In the morning the kitchen smelled of toast.

The cricket bat was on the table.

A child's bat — the right size, proper — real willow, real grip. Beside it, a small doll with yellow hair. Gary stood in the doorway and looked at them.

His father was at the table with his tea and his newspaper. He didn't look up.

Noela was already in her chair. She had the doll in her lap. Her face had nothing to give away.

Gary came in and sat down. He looked at the bat. He picked it up. It was heavier than he expected — heavier than a toy, heavier than something you're given to keep you quiet — and the weight of it settled into his hands exactly the way real things do.

His father turned a page.

Gary set the bat on the table. He looked at his own hands. He looked at the back of the newspaper.

"Thank you," he said.

His father turned another page.

Outside, the Ponsonby sparrows were doing what they did. The kitchen was full of ordinary morning light, the kind that lands on everything equally and asks nothing of it.

Gary held the bat under the table where his father couldn't see. He held it in both hands and he said nothing else, and the newspaper turned another page, and the sparrows went on, and everything was exactly as it always was.
