Chapter Eight: First Fifteen


The boots were half a size too big and the socks didn't help.

Gary had stuffed the toes with newspaper that morning, standing in the bathroom while the house was still dark, trying to make borrowed things fit. It hadn't worked. He could feel the heel slipping with every step across the wet field and there was nothing to be done about it now.

He stood at the edge of the group and watched the other boys warm up and understood, with the particular clarity that arrives when there is no longer any point in pretending otherwise, that his body did not belong here.

These were not boys like him. They were sixteen and seventeen and they had the density of something that had been made specifically for this purpose — thick through the shoulder, low through the hip, moving across the wet grass with an ease that had nothing to do with thought. They'd been doing this since they were eight. Some of them had been doing it since they were six. You could see it in the way they caught — both hands, natural, the ball arriving in their grip like something expected. Gary had never played club rugby. He had never wanted to.

Noel had said: "Lou made First XV. University level now."

He hadn't needed to say the rest.

The coach — Caniston, PE, a man with a jaw that looked like it had been formed under pressure — looked Gary up and down in a way that said everything and then arranged his face into something neutral.

"Thompson," he said. "You played club?"

"No, sir."

"Intermediate grade?"

"No, sir."

Caniston looked at him for a moment longer, then looked at something over Gary's shoulder. "Right. We'll see what you've got."

—

What Gary had was this: he could catch, mostly, when the pass was straight and he had time. He could not catch when the pass was low or wide or arrived at speed, and in a rugby trial most passes were at least one of these things. He fumbled the first. He held the second but the throw was poor and the boy next to him gave him a look that didn't require translation. He held the third cleanly and felt a brief loosening in his chest that lasted until the next drill.

The tackling was worse.

The boy who ran at him the first time weighed seventy kilograms if he weighed a pound and he ran with his hips low and his knees driving and Gary tried to get his arms around him the way he'd seen it done and what happened instead was that he went sideways and then backwards and his shoulder hit the grass and he lay there for a moment looking at the grey Morrinsville sky.

He got up.

He knew his father was watching. He had known it since he crossed the car park. He had not looked in that direction and he would not look in that direction because looking would mean acknowledging that he knew, and acknowledging that he knew would mean that this — all of this, the borrowed boots, the grass in his mouth, the boy who'd just run through him like he wasn't there — was a performance. Which it was. Which it had always been. Which was the only way Gary Thompson knew how to inhabit any room his father arranged for him.

He got back into the line.

The scrums were last. Eight boys locked together in that particular violence, the weights and angles of it, the total commitment required from a body that had given over completely to the task. Gary went in where he was put and the weight came down on him and his knees buckled and then his back went wrong and the whole thing collapsed on top of him and he came out from under it with grass on his face and a feeling behind his ribs like something had shifted.

Caniston's whistle.

"Thanks for coming out, Thompson. We'll be in touch."

Gary walked toward the car park.

—

The drive home took eleven minutes. Gary counted them. His father did not speak for ten of them. He sat in the driver's seat with both hands on the wheel and his eyes on the road and the silence had a pressure to it, the particular pressure of a silence that is not absence but presence — something filling the car that had no name but was felt in the stomach and the jaw and the back of the neck.

At the intersection before the house, his father spoke.

"You looked pathetic out there."

He said it the way you state a fact. The way you tell someone the time. His eyes stayed on the road.

The backhand came at the same moment — measured, accurate, the flat of his hand against Gary's cheek, snapping his head sideways against the window. The glass was cold. Gary kept his eyes forward and felt his face arranging itself into nothing.

"Caniston'll be telling his mates."

Gary said nothing.

They turned into the drive. His father got out of the car and went inside without looking back and Gary sat in the passenger seat for a moment with his hand pressed flat against his thigh and his jaw working very slightly, finding and then losing and then finding the stillness again.

Then he got out and went in.

—

He kept his head down in the morning. It was possible to move through the school in a way that didn't invite scrutiny if you knew the routes and the timings, and Gary knew them. He went to his desk and opened his textbook and looked at it without reading it.

One of the rugby boys said something across the room. Gary didn't look up. He heard his name and the word *Caniston* and something else and then the laughter that follows, the brief bright particular laughter of people sharing something at someone else's expense.

He kept his eyes on the page.

It was Heather who made him look up.

She was across the room and slightly to his left and she wasn't part of the laughter — he was aware of that before he was aware of anything else about her, the way she'd stayed outside it, the way her face had stayed still. When he looked up she was already looking at him.

It wasn't pity. He knew what pity looked like — it had an element of relief in it, the small involuntary pleasure of finding yourself on the better side of someone else's misfortune. This was something else. It was direct and quiet and it stayed on him for a moment without flinching and then she looked back at her textbook.

That was all.

Gary looked back at his own textbook. The words on the page were still illegible.

He walked home at the end of the day with his collar up against the Morrinsville wind and the mark above his eye beginning to colour properly now, purple coming into the yellow, the full geography of it settling into its final shape. He thought about the scrums and the boots and Caniston's face. He thought about his father's hands on the wheel. He thought about eleven pounds in a post office and a reputation for honesty that bought nothing.

He thought about the look.

Not the pity he'd expected. Not the turning away. Something that had seen the mark above his eye and the set of his shoulders and the stillness he wore like a second skin — and had looked at all of that without flinching. Without making it into something.

He didn't know her name yet. He would learn it later that week, from the roll when the teacher called it, and the name would settle into his memory alongside the look — both of them precise, specific, the kind of thing that stays.

He walked home in the cold and he kept it carefully, the way you keep something you're not sure you deserve to have.
