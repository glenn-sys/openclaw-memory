Chapter Fifteen: The Dark Room


The lounge was dark except for the television.

Not on — the standby light. A single red point in the corner of the room, doing nothing. Outside, the North Shore going about its Friday night. Cars. Someone's dog.

I'd walked home from Chris Jones's place — two streets over — with six or eight cans in me, some cheap vodka on top of that, a few hits of a joint on the walk. The party had ended the way things ended at Chris's: his mother had gone for Michelle's boyfriend, the neighbours had called the police, everyone had split. A domestic. Cops on the doorstep was just the final punctuation on a night that had been building to something all evening.

Chris's house was like that. It was the kind of house that attracted a certain energy — no functioning adults, a mother who drank, a rotating cast of strange men coming and going as boarders since Bruce had walked out and left the kids to it. We all gravitated there because it had no rules, and no rules felt like freedom, and at sixteen freedom was the only thing any of us were actually looking for. Some nights it was. Some nights a mother went for someone's boyfriend at eleven o'clock and the police arrived and you walked home through the dark with your collar up, wondering what you'd been thinking.

I'd walked through the North Shore dark and arrived at my own front door with the particular courage that comes not from a drink or two but from the full obliteration of your own better judgment — which is the only kind of courage a sixteen-year-old who has been waiting his whole life to say something has ever really had access to.

My father was in the armchair.

I almost missed him. He was so still. No lamp on, no television, just the dark and the red standby light and a man sitting in a chair the way men sit when they've run out of reasons to move. The glass on the side table caught what light there was — amber, a finger left in it. He'd been there a while.

I said: "Dad."

He didn't startle. Just turned his head. His face in the dark was unreadable, which was usual, except the quality of it was different tonight. Not the closed-off face, the face that meant he was elsewhere in his head and you were an interruption. Something else. The face of a man who had also been sitting with something he couldn't say.

"You're late," he said.

"Yeah."

I stood in the doorway. The alcohol in me was making the room slightly imprecise around the edges — not spinny, not yet, but that particular softening where the hard edges of things go compliant and your own voice sounds like it's coming from slightly further away than your mouth. I was aware of my own heartbeat. I was aware that I was going to do something I had never done before, that I was going to do it because I had two cans in me and the night outside was still going and something about the dark and the stillness of him in the chair made the usual distance seem, briefly, crossable.

I came in and sat on the sofa. The cushions were the wrong height — too soft, bought for how they looked rather than how they felt. Everything in the Hillcrest house was like that. Chosen for what it said about who we were rather than for comfort.

We sat there for a moment.

The standby light. The glass on the table. The sound of the street.

I said: "Why don't you like me?"

He was quiet.

"Dad." The word felt strange in my mouth, like a word in a language I'd been studying from a textbook without ever speaking aloud. "Why don't you like me. Why didn't you — " I stopped. Started again. "I just want to know why."

The question was sixteen years old. It had been sitting in my chest since before I had language for it, since the VCR, since the primary schools, since every time I'd looked up and found him looking somewhere else. I'd drunk most of a bottle of cheap vodka and more Steinlager than I'd counted at a party that had ended in a domestic two streets over, smoked a joint on the walk home through the North Shore dark, and now I was sitting in the dark asking my father the question I'd been carrying since I was small enough to fit in his arms, which is to say since before I could remember, since before I knew there was a question.

He didn't answer for a long time.

Then: "It's not that I don't like you."

I waited.

"I don't — " He stopped. His hand moved to the glass on the table, picked it up, set it down without drinking. "I don't know how to —" Another stop. Something working in him that wasn't used to working. "My father," he said. "Your grandfather."

Outside, the dog again.

"He never — " Gary stopped a third time and I watched him try to find it, whatever it was he was trying to say, rummaging through decades of careful silence for language that had never been made available to him. "He never. You know. He wasn't — he didn't — "

And then something happened that I had no preparation for.

My father began to cry.

Not the way men cry in films — not quietly, with dignity, a single tear making its point. The way men cry when they have spent forty years not crying and the architecture of it finally gives. The way it sounds when something structural goes. He put his face in his hands and his shoulders moved and the sounds that came out of him were ugly and shapeless and had no composure in them whatsoever and I sat on the sofa two metres away and watched my father come apart in the dark.

He was saying something. Into his hands. I caught pieces of it — *didn't know how*, and *never showed me*, and something that might have been *I'm sorry* or might have been something else entirely, I couldn't hold the words still long enough to know.

I should tell you what I felt.

The honest accounting of it, at sixteen, in that room: I felt ashamed.

Not for myself. For him. Sitting there in the dark with his glass and his armchair and his forty years of careful distance, and now this — this shapeless falling apart in front of his drunk sixteen-year-old son at midnight, everything coming out of him that should have come out twenty years ago in a form that might have been useful to someone. I looked at my father crying in the armchair and I felt contempt rise in me like something physical, something tidal, and underneath the contempt was grief I didn't have the first idea what to do with, and underneath the grief was the terrible small fact that this was what I had been asking for my entire life and it had arrived too late and in the wrong form and I did not know how to receive it.

I stood up.

He was still crying when I left the room. I didn't say anything. I walked down the hall to my bedroom and I closed the door and I lay on my bed with the ceiling doing what ceilings do when you've had enough vodka and the room is dark and I listened to the sounds from the lounge, which eventually stopped, and then there was just the house and the dog somewhere outside and the party still going two streets over — or not the party, that was over, just the aftermath of it, whatever people do after the police have been and gone and Chris's mother has been put to bed and the night has become something quieter and stranger than it was.

I didn't go back.

---

I'm forty-six. I've thought about that night more times than I can count and I still don't know what I should have done instead. Sat with him, maybe. Said something. Reached across the two metres of carpet between the sofa and the armchair and — what. Offered what. I was sixteen and I was properly drunk and I had been asking for that moment since before I could form the question, and when it came I turned away from it because the form it arrived in — my father undone, my father insufficient, my father needing something from *me* — was the one form I had never been prepared for.

I understand now what he was trying to say. He was telling me the truth, which was the first true thing he'd said to me in as long as I could remember, which was that he did not know how to love me because no one had ever shown him how. That Noel Thompson had stood in every doorway of his childhood and taken up all the available space, and what grows in the absence of warmth grows crooked, and Gary had grown crooked, and here he was at forty trying to say so at midnight in the dark.

It was the closest he ever came.

I walked away.

I have spent a long time understanding that what I did in that room was learn something. Not consciously. Not as a lesson I could name. But the thing I did — standing up, feeling the shame of his weakness, leaving a man alone in the dark with his grief — I had been trained for it my whole life. Trained by the very man who was crying. I didn't cause his grief. I received it, and returned it, in the only language I'd been given.

That's not absolution. I'm not looking for absolution.

Dana is eighteen. She's leaving for university in the autumn and I don't know what she'll find out there or who she'll find it with. I don't know what she's already learned from watching me — what she took in without knowing she was taking it in, what she'll recognise in someone else's face before she knows why it feels familiar.

I know what it cost him to sit in that room.

I know because I know what it cost me to walk away from it. And if she ever needs to ask someone a version of the same question — if she ever has that much riding on a single conversation — I need to have already told her everything. Not so she doesn't get hurt. You can't protect people from that. But so she knows what hurt looks like from the inside. So she knows what she's looking at when she sees it.

I left my father in that room and I never went back. Two years later I'd be gone from the house entirely. Ten years after that he'd be sitting in a hotel room in Wellington that looked nothing like the Hillcrest lounge, and the distance between us would have grown so large it had become simply the landscape — the normal geography of our relationship, so familiar neither of us could see it as loss anymore.

He cried in the dark.

I stood up and left.

That's the whole of it. I don't have anything larger to make of it than that. Sometimes things happen and they are exactly as small and as enormous as they appear, and the only honest thing you can do is write them down the way they were.

My father sat in an armchair in the dark.

I walked down the hall and closed my door.

Neither of us ever mentioned it again.
