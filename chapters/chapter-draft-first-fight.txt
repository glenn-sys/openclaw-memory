Chapter [X]: The Small Hill

There is a hill at the front of the primary school in Glenfield. Hill is probably too grand a word for it — a rise, really, a small mound of grass between the classrooms and the footpath, the kind of thing a groundskeeper once shaped for reasons nobody can remember. To a six-year-old it felt like geography that mattered.

This is where Dylan waited.

I don't remember what started it. I'm not sure it needed a start — some things at that age don't require a cause, just proximity and the particular electricity that moves between boys who haven't yet learned to contain themselves. Dylan was nine. Three years older. To me that gap felt like the distance between species. He was already in a different country — taller, harder, with a face that had already been arranged by things I couldn't see yet.

Every kid at school knew which house was his. You knew it the way you know things before you have the words for them — the overgrown section, the windows that didn't look right, the feeling when you rode your bike past that you should keep moving. I understand now what I was reading. Then I just understood: this kid comes from somewhere difficult, and difficult has migrated into him.

He hit me first. Or pushed me first. The sequence has blurred but the ground hasn't — that grass, that particular angle of the hill, the way the world tilted when I went down.

Here is what I remember clearly: getting up.

Not why. Not some conscious decision I made about dignity or pride or refusing to lose. Just — up. The way a body does it before the mind has weighed in. Back on my feet, facing him, and then down again, face in the grass, his weight on my back, the smell of dirt and dry lawn filling my nose. He pinched me. Pushed my face down. There was something almost bored about it — like he'd done this before, like he knew how it ended.

I got up again.

The second time hurt more than the first. My eye was watering — not crying, I was very clear with myself about that, it was the eye's fault, the grass, the wind — and my chest had that hollow feeling that happens before tears you're not going to let out. He was bigger. He was older. He had three years of gravity on me.

I got up a third time.

I don't know what I looked like to the kids watching. I don't know if there were kids watching. There might as well not have been — the world had contracted to the size of that hill, the two of us, the grass, the getting up.

This time I swung properly.

Connected, too. Right on his cheekbone — I felt it in my wrist and knuckles, a solid thing, real, and for one moment I saw it register on his face. Not pain exactly. Surprise. The slight widening of his eyes that meant: you're still here. You're actually still here.

Then he put me on the ground for the third time and the lesson concluded.

I was still there when the teacher arrived. I don't remember which teacher. I remember being pulled upright, and the adult voice doing what adult voices do in these situations — the loud neutral tone of someone managing a situation they arrived too late to prevent. Dylan was already gone or being walked away. I stood on the hill and my knees had grass stains and I had not won.

But I'd hit him.

Once, properly, on the cheekbone, and I'd watched it matter.

---

I've thought about that hill more times than makes sense for something that lasted maybe four minutes on a Tuesday in 1986.

What I keep returning to isn't the fight. It's the getting up. The third time especially — when any reasonable calculation would have said: stay down, it's over, you've already lost twice and the margin isn't improving. Some part of me hadn't done that calculation. Some part of me was just already standing.

I didn't understand Dylan then. Understand him better now — a nine-year-old from a home that had already marked him, carrying weight in his body that he hadn't asked for, doing to smaller kids what was probably being done to him somewhere else. The cruelty wasn't personal. That's the thing about hurt that gets passed down — it doesn't travel with instructions. It just moves.

But I was six and I didn't know that. I just knew a bigger kid had put me in the grass and I'd gotten up and I'd connected once, clean, before the end.

That punch. That one shot on the cheekbone before he finished me off.

I've been chasing that feeling for forty years. The clarity of it. The moment when something you aimed actually landed. Not the winning — I didn't win. But the proof that you were still in it. That the ground couldn't hold you. That you got up enough times for it to cost him something, even if only his surprise.

I know where it came from.

Noel Thompson never walked away from a fight in his life. Not once, as far as anyone who knew him could remember — not from the pub fights in Freeman's Bay, not from the harder things that happened behind closed doors, not from anything the world put in front of him. He met it. That was the only mode he had. It served him and it destroyed him in roughly equal measure and somewhere in the bloodline, without anyone asking my permission, it arrived in me.

At six years old on a grass hill in Glenfield, I had no idea I was carrying him.

I just got up.
