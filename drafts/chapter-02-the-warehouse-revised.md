# Chapter Two: The Warehouse

---

I need to tell you where it started. Not with me on the floor — that comes later, or rather, it already came, in the first pages of this book, the way certain things arrive before you understand them. No. It started somewhere colder. Somewhere so far from that lounge floor that the connection looks, at first, like coincidence.

The Shetland Islands sit at the top of the world in the way that places sit at the top of the world when no one chose them — when geography simply ran out and people stayed because leaving was harder than enduring. They are not beautiful in the way that softens you. They are beautiful in the way that grinds. The wind comes off the North Sea without interruption, without mercy, because there is nothing between the islands and the Arctic to slow it down. The light in winter arrives late and leaves early, a few hours of grey consideration before darkness returns to finish what it started. The land is peat and stone and salt grass, and the people who lived on it for generations developed the particular quality of things shaped by weather — not broken, but compressed. Hardened in ways that don't always look like strength from the outside.

Noel Thompson's father grew up in that compression.

I don't know his name. Or rather, I know it but it was never spoken with anything but contempt, so it has no weight in this story — it is just a name, a label on something that leaked. What I know is this: he was a Shetlander who followed a fishing boat to Australia sometime in the 1920s, the way men follow boats when the land stops offering them anything. He made it to Brisbane, then south, then across the Tasman to New Zealand in a series of moves that looked like ambition from the outside and were probably desperation from the inside. He settled in Freeman's Bay, Auckland, in the particular way that drifters settle — not choosing a place so much as running out of momentum.

He drank. Chronically, fluently, with the commitment of a man who had found the one thing he was genuinely good at. He went to prison for small things — petty theft, drunk and disorderly, the minor crimes of a man who couldn't organise himself into anything larger. He was a terrible father in the specific way that absent men are terrible fathers: not violent so much as simply not there, and when he was there, reduced to something that required managing rather than following.

He carried something from the Shetlands into that house in Freeman's Bay. Not in any mystical sense — I'm not interested in mysticism, only in pattern. He carried it in his body, in the particular shape of his helplessness, in the way he reached for a bottle the way other men reach for solutions. The islands had compressed him and the compression had cracked something, and what leaked out of the crack he passed on to his sons before he could stop himself, before he even knew he was doing it.

His sons watched him and decided they would not be him.

That decision — made by two boys in a Freeman's Bay house that had fewer rooms than a decent warehouse — is where the real story starts.

---

The sack was on the floor and Noel was crouched over it.

Jute. It had the smell of all jute — lanolin and seawater and the long dark of a ship's hold — but this one had something else underneath. Sharper. He pressed two fingers against the weave and leaned close. Faintly chemical. You wouldn't catch it unless you knew what you were after.

He stood and rolled his right hand into a fist. The knuckles had gone stiff again. The sailor from Thursday night had a harder jaw than he'd looked to have. Noel flexed the hand once and set it at his side.

Across the warehouse floor, Gordon was lifting another sack from the bale. He was the younger brother — not small, but built differently to Noel, a frame that still had room in it for softness that Noel's didn't. He grunted with the weight and set the sack down and straightened.

"This the lot?"

"This is the lot." Gordon pulled at his collar. "Forty-two bales. Same as last time."

Noel walked the length of the floor. Late afternoon, the light coming through the high windows at the north end — three intact, one cracked corner to corner and held together with a strip of tape gone brown with age. Below the windows, the wall was marked with a watermark in the rough shape of a map of somewhere imaginary. He'd looked at that stain a hundred times and never decided what it was a map of.

Outside: Freeman's Bay doing what it always did. A cart horse on the road. Someone's radio through a window — a woman's voice rising and then cut off. The gulls over the harbour. He'd grown up three streets over from here, in the house with fewer rooms than this warehouse had windows. The sounds hadn't changed. He and Gordon both had.

They had spent their childhoods watching their father come apart in that house — slowly, then quickly, with the particular momentum of a man who had chosen the direction of his falling and committed to it. Noel had looked at him at fourteen the way you look at a stain you mean to understand before you scrub it out. He had felt not pity but a cold contempt, clean and clarifying, like cold water on a cut. He would be a strong man. A man who was respected because he had earned it. He would not be excused and he would not explain himself and he would not be like that.

Gordon had come to the same conclusion, following Noel there the way he had followed him everywhere since he could walk. He was softer than Noel — that was simply true, the way it's true of a second-born who has had someone to follow. He still asked questions where Noel made decisions. He still weighed things, hesitated, looked for the angle Noel had already chosen. But he was not weak. Noel had watched him handle men who needed handling. The softness was in his face, not in what he did when things required doing.

They had built this together. That was the fact of it. Not Noel's alone, and Gordon knew it, and Noel knew Gordon knew it, and that was the arrangement between them.

"Forty-two's not enough," Noel said.

Gordon pulled a face. "It's what we've got."

"I know what we've got. I'm telling you what we need." He kicked the sack lightly — a testing kick, the kind a man gives a tyre. "Hartley's are running forty a week through College Hill. We move forty-two in a month, we're nobody."

"We're starting up." Gordon spread his hands. "You have to start somewhere."

"Aye." Noel looked at him. "But where you start is where people put you."

Gordon was quiet for a moment. Then: "So we go back to Carmichael."

"Carmichael wants more than I'm giving him."

"What does he want?"

"He wants in."

The word landed and sat. Gordon looked at the bales stacked against the far wall. He seemed to be doing arithmetic that had nothing to do with numbers. This was the difference between them: Gordon thought before he agreed. He would come around — Noel knew that — but he needed to travel the distance himself.

"What kind of in?" he said finally.

"Partnership. His name on it."

"And you said?"

"I said I'd think about it."

"There's other suppliers."

"Two. Henderson's drinking himself sideways, won't last the year. McLay won't move anything he can't account for in a ledger." Noel's jaw moved. "Carmichael has the import line. We need the volume."

Gordon nodded slowly. "So we give him what he wants."

Noel crouched back down over the sack. He ran his thumb along the seam at the bottom — stitching uneven, hurried, someone closing this one fast. He pinched the corner and pulled. Two stitches parted. He put the opened seam under his nose.

Definitely chemical. Not wool processing. Something else.

He set it back down exactly as he'd found it.

"Not yet," he said.

"What are you waiting for?"

Noel stood. Through the cracked window, gulls were lifting off something in the road, spiralling up on the updraft from the harbour, white against the white of the late afternoon sky.

"There's stuff going through here that isn't ours to know about yet." He looked at the sack. "Carmichael's been moving it for two years. Same trucks as the wool. Same manifests, same drivers. He doesn't hide it from the right people."

Gordon's face went careful. "What kind of stuff?"

Noel looked at him for a long moment.

"The kind that needs special handling."

He said it the way a man says the last line of a story — not the end of the story, but the limit of what he'd say tonight. He picked up the sack, dropped it with the others, and began doing up his coat.

"Get the doors," he said.

Gordon got the doors.

Outside, the cart horse was gone. The street was gravel and mud, the late light pulling long shadows from the fence posts down to the water. Noel lit a cigarette, cupping the match against the harbour wind, and for a moment the flame lit his face from below — jaw, cheekbones, the particular set of his eyes — and then it went out and the dark came back.

He didn't think about his father. He almost never did. That was the discipline.

He walked home.

---

Forty years later, roughly. Give or take.

I was on my lounge floor and I was twelve years old and I was dying, or close enough that the body doesn't know the difference. What was in me had come a long way to get there — across an ocean, through two generations, through a warehouse in Freeman's Bay where two brothers stood over a sack of jute and decided what kind of men they were going to be.

Nobody told me that. I had to work it out myself. It took most of my life.
