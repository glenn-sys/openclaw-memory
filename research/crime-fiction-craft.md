*A technical knowledge base for editing and providing publishable-standard feedback on crime fiction, with specific application to a 1940s New Zealand setting.*

## DOMAIN 1: THE MASTERS — TECHNICAL ANALYSIS

### TANA FRENCH — *In the Woods*, Dublin Murder Squad

#### POV Discipline — Deep First Person and Interiority Mechanics

French writes first person that is not confessional but *immersive*. The distinction matters. Confessional first person (Agatha Christie's Hastings, many cozies) creates distance: the narrator tells us what happened. French's narrators *live* what happens, and the telling is inseparable from the experiencing.

Her central device is the **unreliable present tense of memory**: her narrators tell the story retrospectively, but French never lets the reader feel safe in that retrospection. Rob Ryan in *In the Woods* opens with: *"What I warn you to remember is that I am a detective."* This is not a throat-clearing preamble — it is an active warning that functions as misdirection. He is warning us about himself.

**Interiority mechanics — what French actually does:**

1. **Physical sensation as emotional proxy.** French rarely says "I felt afraid." Instead, sensation registers — a tightening in the chest, the way attention snaps too fast to a sound, the specific quality of cold. She writes: *"Something moved in my mind, a soft half-remembered thing like a hand slipping under a door."* The simile does double work: it describes the sensation AND plants the woods imagery that haunts Rob throughout.

2. **The withheld thought.** French's narrators interrupt themselves, change subject mid-thought, qualify endlessly. This is psychological realism about trauma: people don't think clearly about the things that damage them. A reader who pays attention notices the evasions before they're explained. The narrator circles trauma the way a tongue circles a broken tooth.

3. **Telling detail chosen by the narrator's wound.** What the narrator *notices* in a scene reveals their damage without the narrator knowing it. Rob Ryan notices which exits are in every room. He registers threat-geometry before he registers human warmth. The reader understands this as residual survival programming before Rob consciously acknowledges it.

4. **Free indirect discourse for interiority.** French blurs the boundary between narrator's voice and narrator's thoughts without using "I thought." The effect: the prose IS the thought, not a report of a thought.

#### Dread Through Accumulation

French's suspense technique is almost anti-thriller: she builds dread through *weight* rather than incident. Specific methods:

- **The wrong detail.** In an otherwise ordinary scene description, one detail doesn't fit — is too deliberate, too specific, too noted by the narrator. It registers subliminally before it registers consciously.
- **The repeated motif.** The woods in *In the Woods* appear in weather, in light quality, in the way sounds reach characters from outside. Repetition of a motif without explanation creates a sense that the environment itself has memory.
- **The held breath.** French slows her prose as tension rises, NOT the opposite. More subordinate clauses, more qualification, more present-tense layering of sensation. The reader slows down because the text slows down, and in that slowing, dread accumulates.
- **The false safety.** She establishes warm, mundane scenes (office banter, pub conversations) that feel genuinely pleasant — and then the dread of the underlying investigation bleeds back in. The contrast is what creates the effect.

#### Setting as Psychological Landscape

The woods in *In the Woods* are not described as *scary*. French describes them as *beautiful, ancient, indifferent*. This is more threatening than horror-movie darkness. Her woods predate human meaning; they don't care. She describes: *"The wood was lovely in the early morning, full of birdsong and pale green light filtering through the leaves, and I hated it with every cell of my body."* 

The key technique: **setting filtered through a damaged consciousness** reveals character while doing location work. The setting doesn't carry the emotion — the character's *response* to it does. French never writes: "The woods felt threatening." She writes the woods as they are, and then puts Rob in them, and his reactions tell us everything.

#### Sentence Rhythm

French's sentences have a characteristic architecture: she loads subordinate clauses to build weight, and then she cuts. The short sentence after a long sequence is where the punch lands.

Example (synthesised from her style): *"We worked through the evidence in the way we always did, methodical and careful and half-hoping someone would call it off or declare the whole thing a mistake, hoping the way you hope when you already know it isn't a mistake, when the quality of the silence in the room tells you something irreversible has happened — and then Cassie found the shoe."*

The final clause — six words — does the work because of the weight before it. 

French goes long when a character is:
- Processing something they don't want to face
- Observing a scene in detail (the accumulation of detail = consciousness at work)
- Moving through memory

She cuts short when:
- The emotional blow lands
- A decision is made
- A truth breaks through the narrator's defences

#### Exposition Without Feeling Like Exposition

French embeds procedural/contextual exposition in scene *conflict*, not in summary. Her detectives explain things to each other not in briefings but in arguments, in doubts, in moments where one detective is trying to persuade another. The reader learns the procedure while watching a relationship.

She also uses **the new pair of eyes** technique: Rob Ryan, working a case that connects to his own childhood, is constantly re-seeing Dublin/the Murder Squad from the position of someone who needs to explain it. This legitimises exposition — he's orienting himself as much as the reader.

#### Unreliable Narrator — What She Withholds and When

French's unreliability is not the Gone Girl variety (active deception). It is **self-deception** — and this is harder to construct because the writer must know where the truth is while the narrator doesn't. The mechanics:

- The narrator presents their interpretation as fact, not interpretation. Rob Ryan doesn't say "I thought she was fine." He says "She was fine." The reader who is paying attention will catch the moments when this certainty is undermined by subsequent events.
- Chronological omissions: the narrator's account skips, rushes, elides. The gaps are meaningful — they mark what cannot be faced.
- The narrator overexplains things that don't need explanation (defensiveness) and underexplains things that do (repression). This rhythm is itself a map of the wound.

French explained in interview that she chose NOT to resolve the mystery of what happened to Rob in the woods because resolving it would have required Rob to *remember*, and the point of the book is that he cannot. The unreliable narrator's limitation IS the theme.

#### Emotional Architecture of the Investigation

Each French investigation is structured as an emotional investigation running parallel to the criminal one. The criminal investigation loses ground while the emotional investigation forces resolution. The final revelation of the crime is often less cathartic than the final revelation about the detective.

This structure: 
1. Detective takes case (echoes personal wound)
2. Investigation proceeds, detective's wound is provoked
3. Case intersects with wound more directly
4. Detective fails professionally *because of* the wound
5. Criminal resolution (often partial or ambiguous)
6. Emotional resolution — the detective confronts what they've been avoiding

### DENNIS LEHANE — *Mystic River*, *Gone Baby Gone*

#### Working-Class Boston Voice — Technical Mechanics

Lehane's voice is not achieved through dialect spelling or slang inventory. It is achieved through:

**1. Syntax that reflects oral speech rhythms.** Sentences start mid-thought, double back, qualify themselves the way people do when speaking. *"Jimmy thought about it. About what it meant to know somebody. What it meant that you could be standing in a bar with a guy for twenty years and not know the first thing about him."* This is not grammatically complex — but it moves the way a working-class man's thought moves. Short declaratives, then elaboration.

**2. Specific physical geography as moral marker.** The Flats, the Point, East Buckingham — these aren't just addresses. The characters know which streets are safe to walk, which are theirs, which belong to others. Social geography is moral geography. When Lehane writes a character driving through an unfamiliar neighborhood, the character's alertness tells us everything about class and tribe.

**3. Restraint around interiority.** Lehane's male characters — Jimmy Marcus, Sean Devine, Dave Boyle — don't articulate their inner lives. They observe. They act. The prose reports actions, not feelings. The feelings are inferred by the reader from the actions, which is how Lehane's characters actually process emotion.

#### Externalising Male Emotion Through Action

This is Lehane's signature craft move. Jimmy Marcus, when he grieves, doesn't grieve — he *acts*. He reorganizes his store. He drives somewhere and sits. He talks to a priest not about grief but about sin. The *subject* of the conversation is not the emotion; the emotion is the pressure behind the conversation.

Technical execution:
- A character makes a physical decision that is, in context, obviously emotional: Jimmy staying at the vigil after everyone else goes home.
- Action is described in detail while emotion is absent from the prose: what his hands do, how he stands, what he looks at.
- Other characters register the emotion for the reader: *"Sean watched him and thought he'd never seen a man more completely alone."*

The third-party registration of emotion is key — it keeps the suffering character in their own repression while giving the reader emotional access. It also creates dramatic irony.

#### Structural Technique — Multiple POVs and Information Control

*Mystic River* uses three close-third POVs (Jimmy, Sean, Dave). Lehane's discipline:

- **Each POV has access to different information** — not just different facts, but different *interpretations* of shared facts. Dave knows what happened to him; Jimmy doesn't. Sean knows police procedure; Jimmy doesn't. The reader accumulates perspective from three incomplete pictures.
- **Information withheld from the reader is withheld from the POV character** — Lehane plays fair. We don't know Dave's full story because Dave hasn't fully admitted it to himself. The mystery is inside the character, not cheated by the author.
- **Chapter breaks control revelation.** A chapter ends with a question implied. The next chapter shifts POV — delaying the answer. But the delay is not arbitrary; it is always the natural place for the POV character to enter the scene. Lehane doesn't shift for cheap cliffhangers. He shifts to show us something the current POV *can't* see.

#### Boston as Moral Landscape

Lehane's Boston is not picturesque. It is a landscape of consequences: what happens when people don't leave, when loyalty becomes a cage, when community is also surveillance. The geography reflects the moral argument of the novel.

**Specific technique:** *Mystic River* opens with the three boys and the car and the stranger — and this happens in a specific street. For the rest of the novel, that street means something. Every time a character drives past it or near it, the street carries the weight of what happened there. Lehane doesn't explain this. He lets the reader feel it.

The river itself is moral metaphor — it flows past all of them, runs through the neighborhood, and at the end it carries what it carries. Lehane doesn't make this explicit. The novel is named after it. That's enough.

#### Dialogue and Subtext

Lehane's characters talk about everything except what they're actually talking about. A scene where Jimmy confronts Dave about the murder doesn't say "I think you killed my daughter." It's about a debt. About where Dave was. About a lot of things that are not the question but orbit the question. 

**The mechanics:**
- Characters deflect to subject-adjacent territory (can discuss money, territory, sports — not feelings, not the thing)
- Questions are answered with questions
- False agreements: "Yeah, sure, you're right" used to close a topic, not to concede it
- Sudden silences: Lehane notes when characters stop talking and what they do instead

#### Pacing Toward Violence

Lehane accelerates by *shortening*. Scenes get shorter, chapters get shorter, sentences get shorter. The accumulation of shorter units creates a rhythmic sense of quickening. He also removes interiority as the climax approaches — there's no room for reflection; there's only action. The violence, when it comes, is often brief and almost matter-of-fact. The build-up is longer than the event. This is not anti-climax — it is the truth about violence, which happens faster than the expectation of it.

### GILLIAN FLYNN — *Gone Girl*, *Sharp Objects*

#### Dual/Unreliable Narrator Mechanics

Flynn's unreliable narrator system in *Gone Girl* is architecturally elegant and worth understanding precisely.

**The structure:**
- Nick's first-person present-day narrative: He is withholding, but the withholding appears to be guilt. Flynn engineers this by having Nick omit details that look damning but are actually irrelevant. The reader, primed to suspect him, reads absence as evidence.
- Amy's diary: this is the planted red herring. The reader gets what looks like the victim's private truth — and is therefore more likely to trust it. Flynn exploits the assumption that diaries = unmediated consciousness. Amy's "diary" is, of course, a fabricated document — and Flynn tells us this, but late, after the trust is established.

**The pivot mechanism:**
Flynn signals that Part Two is different with a direct address to the reader that shifts register. Amy's diary voice changes — becomes sharper, more knowing. This is retrospectively legible as the same person, but at a different level of self-disclosure. The reader understands in retrospect that the diary voice was a performance, and the new voice is... also possibly a performance. Flynn never lets the reader fully trust either narrator.

**How she plants information:**
- True clues are seeded in apparently trivial details: the punch bowl, the clue hunt structure, Nick's pattern of evasion.
- False leads are emphasised rather than hidden: Flynn gives the reader too much of certain information, making it seem like confirmation of a theory rather than misdirection.
- The twist is constructed so that re-reading the book, the foreshadowing is obvious. This is the test of good unreliable narrator construction: it must work on first read AND make sense on reread.

#### Dark Female Interiority Without Melodrama

Flynn's psychological damage is rendered without either sentimentalisation or exploitation. Technical methods:

**Specificity.** Flynn never says "Camille was damaged by her childhood." She shows Camille in *Sharp Objects* running her fingers over scars under her clothes, in the same way one adjusts a bra strap — habitually, automatically, as if this is just maintenance. The normalcy of the gesture is what makes it disturbing.

**Dark humour as defence mechanism.** Flynn's narrators are funny. Camille observes her family with a deadpan anthropologist's eye. Amy's Cool Girl speech is satirically brilliant before it's revealing. The wit is not relief from the darkness — it IS the darkness, because people who are seriously damaged often develop devastating observational humour as a survival tool.

**The unrealised disclosure.** A character will describe something horrifying in factual, unemotional terms, as if it's normal. Flynn trusts the reader to be disturbed by the absence of affect rather than its presence.

#### Prose Style — Precision

Flynn's sentences are cut to the minimum. She is ruthless about the verb doing the work. No hedging constructions. The nouns are specific. The rhythm is short-to-medium, with occasional longer moves when she's building dread. She avoids abstractions — everything is concrete.

Example from *Gone Girl*: *"I picture Amy smiling. Not the real Amy — the Perfect Amy in the diary. I always fall for her a little bit."* Three short sentences. First establishes the image. Second undermines it. Third reveals the character's weakness. Total: 18 words.

The rule Flynn embodies: every sentence should be doing exactly one thing, clearly.

#### Twist Architecture

Flynn's twist in *Gone Girl* is not a surprise ending — it is a restructuring of everything that came before. The reader doesn't receive new information; they receive a new frame for existing information. This is why it works:

1. **The twist is planted early enough** that the reader can look back and see it was always there.
2. **The twist doesn't require coincidence** — it emerges from character logic. Amy is exactly the person who would do this.
3. **The twist doesn't resolve everything** — Flynn keeps the reader off-balance post-twist by making neither narrator fully trustworthy in the new configuration.

Bad twist construction: the murderer is someone we've never met, or their motive requires information we didn't have. Flynn's twist: the murderer is the person we thought was the victim, and once you know it, every scene is re-readable as proof.

#### First Page Technique

*Gone Girl* opens: *"When I think of my wife, I always think of the back of her head."* This is a perfect first line because:
- It establishes POV intimately (husband, first person)
- It introduces Amy before she's named
- It is subtly wrong — husbands don't typically think of the back of their wife's head. The wrongness registers subliminally.
- It invites the reader's question: why that image?

*Sharp Objects* opens in violence and dislocation: the protagonist arriving in her home town as an outsider, seeing it with adult eyes. The first page establishes: damaged protagonist, returning home (gothic convention), journalism as licence for investigation, and the specific quality of Camille's damaged voice. Flynn doesn't set up the plot — she sets up the *experience* of reading the book.

### MICHAEL CONNELLY — Bosch Series, *The Lincoln Lawyer*

#### Procedural Authenticity Without Becoming a Manual

Connelly's background as a crime journalist gives him procedural authenticity, but his craft is in using that knowledge selectively. He includes procedure when it:
- Creates obstacles for Bosch (bureaucracy, chain of command, evidence protocols)
- Reveals character (how Bosch conducts himself at a scene tells us who he is)
- Creates authentic pace (investigations are slow and repetitive; making this feel real, not tedious, is skill)

He omits procedure when it would stop the story. Connelly knows what the LAPD's inter-department reporting structure looks like in detail — he doesn't tell you all of it. He tells you the part that matters for this scene, this moment.

**The key principle:** procedure is not colour. It is pressure. The procedure creates constraints that the character must navigate, and it is in that navigation that character is revealed.

#### Spare, Clean Prose — What He Cuts

Connelly's prose is essentially invisible. He writes close-third, past tense, with almost no metaphor and minimal interiority. What he cuts that other writers keep:

- **Adjective stacking.** Connelly uses one adjective, usually precise and specific, never two.
- **Hedging verbs.** No "seemed to," "appeared to," "started to." Actions happen or they don't.
- **Emotional explanations.** Bosch's emotional life is reported in its effects on behaviour. He doesn't feel angry; he pours the coffee down the sink without drinking it.
- **Weather as mood.** Connelly notes weather as Bosch notices it — as a detective noting environmental conditions. Not as atmosphere.
- **Transition paragraphs.** Connelly ends scenes cleanly and starts the next scene in medias res.

#### Chapter Ending Technique

Connelly's chapter endings are consistently one of three types:
1. **The question.** A revelation that raises a new question without answering the current one. The chapter ends at the moment of new uncertainty.
2. **The decision.** Bosch makes a choice. The reader needs to know what happens next.
3. **The ticking clock.** A deadline is established, narrowed, or shifted. Time pressure creates momentum.

Crucially, Connelly's micro-cliffhangers are *fair* — they're not cheap shock cuts. The reader understands why the chapter ended there. The cut has narrative logic.

#### Planting Clues — Visible in Retrospect

Connelly plants clues by **making them invisible through context**. A clue embedded in the middle of a long investigative scene, surrounded by other procedural details of similar weight, will not stand out. The reader processes it as routine investigation. On re-reading, it is obvious.

The technique: the clue must be planted when the character is actively investigating (not in summary), must be surrounded by other real (but irrelevant) investigative detail, and must be readable as either important or unimportant at the time of reading.

Connelly also uses **the false reveal** — he allows Bosch to draw a wrong conclusion from a real clue, which the reader may follow, embedding the correct interpretation of the clue in the reader's understanding while Bosch's interpretation misdirects.

#### The Detective's Wound — Bosch's Damage

Bosch's core damage: abandoned by a mother killed by the powerful, raised in the system, shaped by Vietnam, further damaged by policing. His wound is about justice and its systematic failure. He doesn't work cases out of ambition. He works them because the dead deserve to be heard.

**How the wound drives plot:**
- Bosch takes cases others won't take (cold cases, victims without advocates) because his wound makes him identify with the abandoned.
- Bosch breaks rules specifically when the rules protect the powerful over the vulnerable — which is always the pressure point of his wound.
- Bosch's professional failures are never random. They are always the result of a specific moral choice made from his wound.

This is structural character damage, not decorative. Remove it and Bosch has no reason to do what he does.

### IAN RANKIN — Rebus Series

#### Scottish Voice and Vernacular — Authentic Without Alienating

Rankin doesn't phonetically spell Scottish dialect. He suggests it through:

- **Vocabulary choices.** Words like "outwith," "braw," "wee," "och" — but used sparingly, naturally, not clustered for local colour. They appear when a character would naturally use them, not when Rankin wants to remind the reader where they are.
- **Register.** Edinburgh characters switch registers based on context — formal with authority, vernacular with mates. This code-switching is itself characterisation.
- **The Edinburgh class system.** The city has Old Town/New Town geography that maps to class. Characters who say "Edinburgh" differently than characters from Glasgow, who say it differently than characters from Leith. Rankin knows these distinctions and they inflect his characters' voices without requiring a glossary.
- **What's left unsaid.** Scottish emotional reticence is characterisation, not absence. Rebus doesn't do emotional disclosure. The depth of feeling is in what is *not* said — and Rankin makes sure there is enough context that the absence registers as full.

#### Edinburgh as Moral Backdrop

Rankin draws on Robert Louis Stevenson's Jekyll and Hyde as the organising metaphor: Edinburgh's beautiful surface/dark underside. The New Town — Georgian, rational, wealthy, respectable. The Old Town — labyrinthine, mediaeval, hidden. Rankin maps this geography onto a moral structure: the Establishment's crimes are New Town crimes; the actual violence and poverty are Old Town.

The city in the Rebus novels is *active*. It changes. Rankin sets the books in real time with current political context (devolution, the Scottish Parliament, the banking crisis, Brexit). This means Edinburgh is not a backdrop — it is a document of a society changing around the detective.

#### Rebus's Failures — Structural, Not Decorative

Rebus is an alcoholic, estranged from his family, in constant professional trouble, frequently wrong about people he cares about. But none of this is merely "edgy character texture." Each failure is structurally connected to the novel's themes:

- Rebus's alcoholism is connected to his inability to compartmentalise the horror of what he sees. The drinking is what happens when he can't stop seeing. This means his professional failures and his personal failures come from the same source.
- His contempt for authority means he gets results the official investigation can't — but it also means he breaks things, damages people, acts on instinct that is sometimes right and sometimes catastrophically wrong. Both consequences are real.
- His failures with women and family are not romantic subplot filler. They show the cost of the job — what policing *does* to a person who takes it seriously.

#### Series Character Development

Across the Rebus novels, Rankin ages him in real time — Rebus is younger in *Knots and Crosses* (1987) and approaches retirement in later books. This creates genuine development rather than the static character of most series detectives (Holmes, Poirot). 

The development is not linear improvement. Rebus doesn't get better. He gets more worn, more damaged, but also — paradoxically — more perceptive. His failures teach him, but not in sentimental ways. He keeps making the same *type* of mistake; he just makes it with more self-awareness.

### RAYMOND CHANDLER / DASHIELL HAMMETT — Hardboiled Foundations

#### The Hardboiled Voice — Mechanics

The hardboiled voice is a moral stance before it is a prose style. Marlowe sees the world without illusion: the rich are corrupt, the cops are bought, the innocent are naive. His voice is the product of someone who *knows this* and has decided to function anyway. The wit is armour.

**Technical construction of the hardboiled voice:**

1. **First-person, present tense observation.** The detective narrates what he sees with a precision that is also judgement. *"The man had a face like a Sunday sermon."* Description and evaluation at once.

2. **The Chandler simile.** The famous similes work because they are *surprising and precise at once*. They describe something abstract (mood, quality of attention, emotional atmosphere) through something concrete and unexpected. They must feel inevitable in retrospect.

   What makes them work: the two terms of the comparison share an *emotional* quality, not a physical one. *"As inconspicuous as a tarantula on a slice of angel food cake"* — both the tarantula and the angel food cake are specific, visual images, but what they share is the quality of *wrongness-in-context*. The simile works because it names the quality (wrongness) through two images, neither of which names the quality directly.

   Why imitations fail: imitators use physically similar terms rather than emotionally similar ones. Or they use comparison items that don't have specific visual texture. Or they reach for the same domain as Chandler (animals, food, weather) without finding the right specific items. The key is that the *surprise* of the comparison must be matched by its *rightness* on reflection.

3. **The single telling detail.** Chandler describes a person through one *specific* physical detail: not "a fat man" but "a man who had been fat for so long that his body had stopped apologising for it." The detail tells you what kind of fat man he is — wealthy, comfortable, entitled.

4. **Restraint on sentiment.** Marlowe sees children mistreated, women used, men destroyed — and his narration doesn't sentimentalise this. The horror is in the *matter-of-fact* quality of the narration. The world is like this. It has always been like this. Marlowe is the witness.

#### The Moral Landscape of Noir

Noir's moral structure: the world is corrupt; institutions are compromised; money is the ultimate solvent. The detective's job is not to fix this — it is to *see clearly* within it, and to do the specific right thing in front of him, even knowing it will change nothing structural.

Marlowe repeatedly refuses money, refuses the powerful client, refuses to trade in information or people. This is not idealism — it is the detective's only remaining dignity. He can't clean up Los Angeles. He can refuse to be part of the dirt.

**This moral structure is directly applicable to a 1940s NZ detective.** The same landscape: colonial power structures, institutional corruption, money and respectability protecting the criminal, the detective as the last honest man in an honest profession.

#### Hammett's Restraint

Hammett writes less than Chandler. Where Chandler creates atmosphere and interiority, Hammett reports action. *The Maltese Falcon* is almost behaviorist: we see what characters do and say; we are told almost nothing about what they think or feel. Sam Spade's inner life is opaque — even more opaque than Marlowe's.

What Hammett doesn't explain:
- Why Spade does what he does at the end (turns in Brigid, the woman he may love)
- What Spade actually thinks of Effie
- Whether Spade believes in anything or is purely transactional

This opacity is not a weakness. It creates the reader's need to *interpret* — Spade becomes a Rorschach onto which the reader projects moral understanding. He seems like a cynical professional with a code, but the code is never articulated. It is demonstrated.

Hammett's technique: action-dialogue-action, with minimal interiority. The style is journalistic. The sentences are short and declarative. The effect is a world stripped of editorialising — just events, just people, just what happened.

### DONNA TARTT — *The Secret History*

#### Creating Dread When the Reader Knows the Ending

*The Secret History* opens with the murder. The first line tells you who was killed and that the narrator participated. Then the novel goes backward to explain how. This is **proleptic narration** (flash-forward at the start), and Tartt uses it to create a completely different kind of suspense: not *what will happen* but *how did we get here* and *what does that mean*.

The dread is **retrospective comprehension** rather than forward anticipation. As the reader understands more about the characters and their world, they understand more precisely how the murder was always inevitable — and that understanding is more disturbing than not knowing.

Technical execution:
- **The reader becomes complicit.** By entering Bunny's social world, enjoying his company, understanding his flaws — the reader builds the same relationship the murderers had. When the murder comes, the reader's own prior enjoyment of Bunny is part of the horror.
- **Small foreshadowings are readable only in retrospect.** Tartt plants character beats early that are innocuous on first read but devastating on reread: a specific quality of Julian's teaching, a moment of Henry's coldness, Bunny's moral blindness.
- **The mechanics of inevitability.** The novel shows how reasonable people make a series of small, understandable decisions that accumulate into catastrophe. No single decision is monstrous. The accumulation is.

#### Immersive World-Building Technique

Tartt creates Hampden College (based on Bennington) as a closed, intensely specific world. Her technique:

- **Sensory specificity without tourism.** She describes the cold, the specific quality of Vermont autumn light, the smell of old buildings — but always in service of the characters' emotional experience, not as set-dressing.
- **Hierarchy made visible.** The social world of the Classics group has rigid internal hierarchy — who defers to whom, who sets the conversational agenda, whose aesthetics dominate. This hierarchy is established through specific social detail, not through announcement.
- **The world as moral pressure.** The aesthetics of the world (Greek, classical, beautiful, rigorous) create the conditions for the crime. The murder is, in part, a consequence of the particular moral structure of this world. Environment determines outcome.

#### Prose Rhythm — Long Sentences That Pay Off

Tartt writes long, periodic sentences (subject deferred until the end) that require the reader to hold multiple clauses in mind simultaneously before the main verb arrives. This creates a reading experience of *suspension*, mimicking the emotional state of her narrator (suspended between past and present, between innocence and complicity).

When the long sentence pays off, it is because the accumulated subordinate clauses have loaded the main clause with significance. Without the subordinates, the main clause would be flat. With them, it is weighted.

She alternates these with direct, declarative statements for variety and impact. The same move as French and Connelly: the short sentence after the long earns its punch from the contrast.

## DOMAIN 2: CRAFT MECHANICS — DEEP TECHNICAL

### POINT OF VIEW

#### First Person vs Close Third — Exact Mechanics

**First person:**
- The narrator is a *character* in the story, narrating with their full personality, bias, and limitation.
- Everything is filtered through this consciousness — the reader *only* knows what the narrator knows, and *only* perceives it through the narrator's interpretive framework.
- The unreliable narrator is a natural consequence of first person: all first-person narration is unreliable to some degree, because all consciousness is partial.
- **Crime fiction advantages:** Creates intimacy with the detective; allows genuine mystery (the reader is as limited as the narrator); enables the "detective as moral lens" tradition.
- **Crime fiction limitations:** Cannot show scenes the narrator doesn't witness; creates awkwardness in investigation scenes where the narrator must be everywhere; limits the reader's ability to know things the detective doesn't.
- Best for: psychological crime, unreliable narrator stories, cases where the detective's *perception* is the subject.

**Close Third (Third Person Limited):**
- Narrator is outside the character but tightly attached to their consciousness. 
- Grammatically uses "he/she/they" but reads subjectively — we see through the character's eyes without the character-as-narrator filter.
- Allows slightly more narrative flexibility: the author can manage what the reader notices without the character noticing it.
- **Crime fiction advantages:** Can show multiple POV characters in separate chapters (Lehane's *Mystic River*); allows the author more control over tone than first person; slightly easier to manage exposition.
- **Crime fiction limitations:** Easier to accidentally slip into distant narration; less immediately intimate than first person; the "author voice" can intrude.
- Best for: multi-POV crime novels; procedurals with ensemble casts; cases where a degree of authorial distance serves the material.

**The key distinction:** In first person, the character *tells* the story. In close third, the author *shows* the story through the character's eyes. The discipline is different. First person requires a consistent narrative voice. Close third requires consistent perspectival discipline.

#### Deep POV — Getting Inside Without "She Thought/Felt"

Deep POV removes the narrator layer entirely. Instead of:
> *She thought the room looked wrong. She felt uneasy.*

Deep POV writes:
> *The room was wrong. Something was off — the chair pulled back from the table at an angle, the glass set in the centre of the coaster rather than at the edge, the way people set things when they're being careful about fingerprints.*

The second version:
- Reports the same conclusion (room is wrong)
- Shows the *reasoning process* rather than announcing the conclusion
- Allows the reader to reach the conclusion with the character
- Removes the authorial layer ("she thought")
- Gives the observation specific texture (chair, glass, coaster, fingerprints)

**The rule:** If you can delete "she thought" or "she noticed" from a sentence and the sentence still works, it's deep POV. If the sentence requires the "she thought" to make sense, you haven't gone deep enough.

**Filter words to eliminate:** *saw, heard, noticed, felt, thought, wondered, realised, decided, believed, knew.* Not because these words are always wrong — but because they signal that the author is standing between the reader and the character's experience.

Correct use: *"He felt the gun kick in his hand."* — Here "felt" is a physical sensation, not a cognitive filter. It's appropriate.

Incorrect use: *"He felt that something was wrong."* — This should be: *"Something was wrong."*

#### POV Discipline — What Breaks the Contract

POV contract: the reader trusts that everything they receive is limited to what the POV character can perceive and know.

**Violations:**
- **Head-hopping:** moving into another character's consciousness within the same scene without clear break. Even a single sentence from another perspective breaks the reader's trust and creates confusion. *"John knew she was lying. Sarah watched him try to read her face."* — this is head-hopping: "John knew" is John's interiority; "Sarah watched" is Sarah's. If we're in John's POV, we can't know what Sarah is experiencing.
- **Omniscient intrusion:** The narrator tells us something the POV character couldn't know: *"He walked into the room, unaware that the gun had been moved."* — this requires a narrator who knows about the gun. If we're in close third from this character, the narrator doesn't have access to this information.
- **Inconsistent knowledge:** The POV character "knows" something in chapter 3 that they didn't know in chapter 1, with no scene accounting for the acquisition of that knowledge.
- **Author's voice bleeding through:** The POV character uses diction or makes connections that are clearly the author's (educated, literary, analytical) rather than the character's (e.g., a semi-literate character making sophisticated literary allusions in their interior monologue).

#### Unreliable Narrators — The Spectrum

**Type 1: Naïve narrator.** The character doesn't understand what they're seeing. *To Kill a Mockingbird*'s Scout reports what she sees; the reader understands more than she does. Crime application: a child or outsider narrator who misinterprets adult crimes.

**Type 2: Self-deceiving narrator.** The character has unconscious blind spots — trauma responses, ideology, love — that distort their interpretation. They're not lying; they can't see. Tana French's Rob Ryan. Crime application: the detective who can't see that the perpetrator is someone they love.

**Type 3: Rationalising narrator.** The character knows they've done something wrong but constructs a narrative that justifies it. Nabokov's Humbert Humbert. Crime application: the culprit-narrator who presents their crime as reasonable.

**Type 4: Actively lying narrator.** Amy Dunne in *Gone Girl*. The character knows they're lying, is constructing a false account for a purpose. Crime application: the killer-narrator misleading an internal inquiry.

**Construction principles for all types:**
- The *author* must know the truth at all times, even when the narrator doesn't.
- The clues to the narrator's unreliability must be present in the text — available to a careful reader on first pass, obvious on reread.
- The unreliability must be *characterologically motivated* — it must arise from who this character is, not from authorial convenience.
- The reveal of unreliability should change the meaning of prior events, not just add new information.

### TENSION AND DREAD

#### Hitchcock's Bomb — The Mechanics of Suspense vs Surprise

Hitchcock described the distinction to Truffaut precisely: Two people sit at a table. A bomb explodes. The audience has fifteen seconds of surprise. 

But if we show the audience the bomb under the table at the START of the scene — then for the entire scene, every word the characters say is charged with the audience's knowledge of what they don't know. The audience has fifteen minutes of *suspense*.

**Applied to crime fiction:**

Surprise = the reader discovers the event at the same time as (or after) the character.
Suspense = the reader knows something the character doesn't — or knows that danger exists even if the specific form is unknown.

**Information control is the entire tool.** The writer is always deciding: what does the reader know at this moment? What does the character know? These are independent variables.

**The three information states in crime fiction:**
1. Reader knows = Character knows (same footing — tension comes from situation)
2. Reader knows > Character knows (Hitchcockian suspense — reader sees the danger the character doesn't)
3. Reader knows < Character knows (mystery proper — reader trying to reconstruct what the character knows)

Most crime novels move between these states. The investigation phase is state 3 (we're figuring it out with or behind the detective). The thriller components move toward state 2 (we can see the trap closing before the character can).

**Dread accumulation** (as distinct from suspense) operates differently: it doesn't require a specific known threat. It requires the reader to feel that threat is present and imminent, even without being able to specify it. French's method. Achieved by:
- Establishing that the world of the novel contains horror (early) without revealing what the horror will be
- Details that are off without being explicable
- The character's subliminal awareness of wrongness (expressed through behaviour, not thought)

#### Micro-Tension at the Sentence Level

Every sentence can carry tension or bleed it. Sources of sentence-level tension:

- **Active constructions:** "He opened the door" creates a forward vector. "The door was opened" removes agency and forward momentum.
- **The specific detail:** Specific detail creates a sense that *this* matters — generic description creates the sense that the writer isn't paying attention.
- **The incomplete — the cut before resolution:** Ending a sentence or paragraph before the action resolves creates a small push forward. *"He reached for the phone —"*
- **The wrong detail in an otherwise normal sentence:** In a list of ordinary observations, one that doesn't fit creates an unsettled quality without the author having to announce that something is wrong.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Short sentences accelerate. Long sentences, paradoxically, can *hold* the reader in a moment of tension — French uses this.

**What kills micro-tension:**
- *"He started to open the door"* — "started to" removes the decisiveness of the action
- *"It seemed like she might have been angry"* — hedging in every direction
- Adverbs that explain the emotion ("she said angrily") rather than embedding it in the action
- Transition paragraphs that fill space rather than advance

#### The False Resolution

The false resolution is a structural device: the investigation appears to be solved. The mystery is wrapped up. A chapter ends at a resolution point. Then the next chapter reveals that the resolution was wrong — a new piece of evidence, a confession that doesn't hold up, a suspect who has an alibi.

**Why it works:** It gives the reader a moment of release, which is then retracted. The retraction creates a more heightened tension than if the resolution had never appeared. The reader has invested in the false resolution; its undoing increases their investment in the real one.

**Construction requirements:**
- The false resolution must be genuinely plausible — it can't be obviously wrong on the face of it, or it won't function.
- The evidence that undoes it must be present in the text before the false resolution (fair play)
- The false resolution should advance the reader's understanding even as it misled: it should reveal something true about the perpetrators or the crime, even if it pointed the wrong way.

### DIALOGUE

#### Subtext Mechanics

Characters communicate on two levels simultaneously: the surface level (what is literally said) and the subtext level (what is actually meant, felt, or being negotiated). The writer's job is to make both levels readable.

**How subtext is created:**
- **Displacement.** Characters talk about something concrete and neutral when they're really negotiating something that can't be said directly. The conversation about money is about power. The conversation about the car is about who's leaving.
- **Excess.** When a character is too emphatic about something that doesn't seem to warrant emphasis, the reader understands the emphasis is about the subtext, not the subject.
- **The avoided topic.** Two people who are circling something they can't discuss will talk about everything except that thing. The gravity of the avoided topic shapes all the adjacent conversations.
- **The interrupted revelation.** A character begins to say the true thing, is interrupted (by another character, by a phone, by their own nerve failing), and the moment passes. The reader knows what they were going to say; the moment is loaded by what didn't happen.

**Dialogue that reveals character under pressure:**
When a character is threatened, challenged, or exposed, their default defensive pattern appears in their speech. Someone who controls through charm begins charming when cornered. Someone who controls through authority becomes authoritative. Someone who deflects through humour makes a joke. The *mechanism* of the defence is character-specific and should be consistent across the novel.

**The difference between realistic dialogue and effective dialogue:**
Real conversation is full of hesitations, incomplete sentences, digressions, misunderstandings, repetitions. Effective dialogue captures the *rhythm and feeling* of real conversation while cutting its redundancy. Every line must either:
- Advance the plot, OR
- Reveal character, OR
- Create/increase tension

If a dialogue exchange does none of these three things, it should be cut, regardless of how natural it sounds.

#### Dialect and Period Voice

The test for dialect: read it aloud. If you stumble on the phonetics, the reader will stumble on the page.

**Rules for period/regional voice:**
- Suggest, don't transcribe. A light touch of period idiom reads as authentic; phonetic transcription reads as mockery.
- Use vocabulary that is period-appropriate but comprehensible: words that carry meaning from context even if the reader doesn't know them, or that can be inferred.
- Avoid anachronism — not just of vocabulary but of attitude. Characters in 1940s NZ shouldn't think or speak in ways that post-date the period. This includes social attitudes that might be difficult for a modern reader but were simply normal at the time.
- Avoid the opposite error: scrubbing the period entirely so the book reads as contemporary fiction with costumes.

**The 1940s New Zealand voice:**
- Influenced by British English but developing distinct local idiom (Sargeson's 1940s fiction documents this)
- Working class/rural speech: laconic, understated, suspicious of articulate emotion
- Middle class: still performing British proprieties, slightly formal
- The word "mate" exists but is not yet the constant NZ marker it becomes post-1970s
- Specific slang of the WWII era (men returned from the Pacific, language of the army, rationing language)
- Absence of Māori loanwords in Pākehā dialogue (this would have been the historical norm, unfortunately, even if Māori vocabulary was in the environment)

**Exposition through dialogue:**
The cardinal rule: no character tells another character something both characters already know solely to inform the reader. This is "As you know, Bob..." dialogue, and readers feel it immediately.

Techniques to embed exposition in dialogue legitimately:
- One character genuinely doesn't know and the other explains (but the explanation must be in-character, not comprehensive)
- Characters argue about interpretation of known facts (the exposition is the subtext of the argument)
- A character explains to a sceptic (the sceptic's questions are the reader's questions)
- A character explains to someone they're trying to impress, mislead, or protect

### SCENE CONSTRUCTION

#### The Swain Scene-Sequel Structure

Dwight Swain (*Techniques of the Selling Writer*) identifies the fundamental structural units of fiction:

**Scene** = the unit of action. Components:
1. **Goal:** What does the POV character want in this scene? Must be specific and immediate (not "to solve the crime" but "to get this witness to talk").
2. **Conflict:** What opposes the goal? (The witness won't talk / is frightened / is lying / is hiding something real)
3. **Outcome:** Does the character achieve the goal? In crime fiction, the outcome almost always fails or partially fails — this is what creates the next scene. The scene ending should be a *disaster* (new problem, worsened situation, revealed complication).

**Sequel** = the unit of reaction. Components:
1. **Reaction:** Emotional response to the scene outcome
2. **Dilemma:** The new problem created by the failed goal
3. **Decision:** The choice the character makes about what to do next (sets up the next scene's goal)

In fast-paced crime fiction, sequels are often compressed or cut almost entirely — the reaction and decision happen in a paragraph between scenes, or even in a single line. Slowing the sequel (more time with reaction and dilemma) slows the pace and increases emotional weight. Cutting it entirely accelerates pace and can create a sense of relentlessness.

#### Entering Late, Leaving Early

The scene should start at the moment of conflict or just before it — not at arrival, not in approach, not with establishing description. Cut the preamble.

Amateur: *"Detective Morrison drove to the Dunbar house and parked outside. He walked up the path and rang the doorbell. Mrs Dunbar answered. 'Detective Morrison,' he said."*

Professional: *"Mrs Dunbar had been crying. She stepped back from the door without greeting him."*

The professional version starts in the middle of a dynamic: someone has been crying, someone else has arrived. The detective's observation of her state is the scene's first conflict beat.

**Leaving early:** End the scene at the point of decision or revelation, not through the exit. The character registers the discovery; cut. Don't write them driving away.

#### The Closing Beat

The closing beat of a scene determines whether the reader continues. It should:
- Raise a question (explicit or implicit)
- Create an obligation (the reader is now *owed* an answer)
- Be a turning point (something has changed; the reader wants to know the consequence)

The closing beat is *not* a summary of the scene. It is not a character reflecting on what we've just read. It is a new element that hooks forward.

### PROSE STYLE

#### Show Don't Tell — The Actual Mechanics

"Show don't tell" is one of the most misapplied principles in writing advice. What it actually means:

**Telling:** The author reports a state, conclusion, or quality. *"She was nervous."*

**Showing:** The author renders the evidence from which the reader infers the state. *"She kept checking the door."*

The showing version is better because:
- It respects the reader's intelligence
- It creates the emotion in the reader by making them *infer* (inferring emotion produces the emotion)
- It creates specificity (we now know she checks the door, which is more textured than "nervous")

**But telling is sometimes right:**
- For minor characters about whom the reader doesn't need texture
- For transitions (summary, not scene)
- When the information is procedural/factual rather than emotional
- When the specific form of "showing" would slow the pace below what the scene requires

The real principle is: **earn the reader's emotional response by rendering experience, not announcing conclusions.** But this doesn't mean never tell. It means never tell what you could *more effectively* show.

#### Weak Verb Constructions

- **"Was" + adjective:** *"He was angry"* → *"He slammed the cup down"*
- **"Started to / began to":** *"She started to speak"* → *"She spoke"* (if she started speaking, she was speaking)
- **"Seemed to":** *"He seemed to hesitate"* → *"He hesitated"* or reconsider whether the uncertainty is earned
- **"Had been":** Past perfect is necessary for sequence of events, but overused as a default. Where possible, find the active past tense.
- **Passive construction bleeding energy:** *"The gun was raised"* → *"He raised the gun"* — unless the agent is unknown, passive removes agency and forward momentum.

**When passive is correct:**
- When the agent is unknown and the unknown is the point (*"The body had been moved"*)
- When the action *done to* the subject is what matters, not who did it
- When distance or formality is the register (official reports, period documents within the fiction)

#### Filtering Language — Why It Distances

*"He saw the blood on the floor."*

This sentence puts the author between the reader and the blood. We are reporting what he saw; we are not *at the blood*. 

*"Blood on the floor."*

This is immediate. We are there.

Every "he saw/heard/noticed/felt" is a layer of remove. In high-tension moments, every layer of remove bleeds tension. Deep POV requires the removal of these filters — not all of them, everywhere, but in every moment where immediacy matters.

#### Writing Weather, Setting, Atmosphere

The rule: **setting description earns its place only when it does character or mood work simultaneously.**

The test: Can you remove this description without losing anything? If yes, it shouldn't be there.

**Technique — filtering setting through POV:**
Never describe the room. Describe what the POV character notices about the room, and make sure what they notice reveals either their own character or the scene's emotional atmosphere.

*"The room was small and neat with blue curtains."* → Neutral, forgettable.

*"Everything in the room was small and neat and still, like a space that had been prepared for inspection. The curtains were blue. He noticed, for no reason, that they matched the wallpaper almost perfectly."* → This is a character with a forensic eye noticing incongruously careful interior decorating — which tells us something about the inhabitants and possibly about the detective.

**Weather in 1940s NZ crime fiction:** The New Zealand landscape is vivid and specific — it should be *felt*, not described. The smell of it (bush, salt, rain on clay), the light quality (southern light is different from northern), the sound. The landscape should be experiential, not scenic.

### CHARACTER

#### The Wound

Every compelling crime protagonist has a **core damage** — a wound that predates the novel and shapes how they engage with everything in it. Not a tragic backstory; a *present wound* that hasn't healed and actively distorts their perception and choices.

**The wound serves three functions:**

1. **Motivation:** Explains why this person does this dangerous, unpleasant job with such commitment. They are, on some level, always trying to solve the crime that wounded them — even when it's a different crime.

2. **Obstacle:** The wound creates blind spots, biases, and emotional vulnerabilities that interfere with the investigation. The detective who can't trust people will miss important confessions. The detective who idealises women will be manipulated. The detective whose wound is about authority will break rules at exactly the wrong moment.

3. **Theme:** The wound connects the detective's personal story to the novel's thematic concerns. Bosch's wound is about justice and abandonment — and that's what every Bosch novel is about. French's Rob Ryan's wound is about memory and self-knowledge — and that's what *In the Woods* is about.

**The wound should be:**
- Specific (not "troubled childhood" but the specific event and its specific sequelae)
- Present (not resolved, not healed, active in every scene)
- Connected to the plot (the case should push on the wound)
- Not announced (revealed through behaviour and pattern, not backstory dumps)

#### Character Through Choice Under Pressure

Character is revealed by decisions made under constraint. Not by description, not by backstory, not by other characters' opinions.

The pressure must be genuine — the character must have something real to lose in any direction. Then watch what they choose. Moral character is revealed only when the choice has moral weight.

**Amateur technique:** Describe the character's personality ("Sarah was stubborn and determined"). 

**Professional technique:** Put Sarah in a scene where she has to keep going after a reasonable person would stop, where stopping would be the sensible choice — and show her not stopping.

#### Writing the Villain/Antagonist

The genuinely threatening antagonist is not evil but *understandable*. Their logic is coherent from inside their worldview; their methods are chosen and effective; they are not cartoonish.

**What makes an antagonist threatening:**
- They are *capable* — they have done the thing they're threatened with, and can do it again
- Their worldview is internally consistent, even if monstrous from outside
- They have something to lose, which makes them dangerous
- They are specific: their violence or cruelty has a particular quality, not generic darkness

**The antagonist in 1940s crime fiction:**
Period crime fiction allows for antagonists whose monstrousness is *institutional* or *systemic* rather than individual: the antagonist can be a system, a class, a set of values — with individual agents. This is richer and more disturbing than the individual psychopath.

## DOMAIN 3: PERIOD CRIME FICTION

### HISTORICAL AUTHENTICITY

#### Period Detail Without the History Lesson

The rule: **research is for the author, not the reader.**

The author needs to know everything. The reader needs to know what matters for *this scene, this moment, this character*. The author's job is to select the details that do double work — that create period atmosphere AND serve character or plot simultaneously.

**A detail that only establishes period:** *"The 1941 Ford V8 sat in the driveway."* — This tells us it's WWII era. It does nothing else.

**A detail that establishes period AND character AND tension:** *"The Ford V8 in the driveway still had the requisition notice under the windscreen wiper. The petrol wasn't in it to take it anywhere. But someone had cleaned the windows."* — We're in the period (requisition), we know about the rationing/scarcity, and we know something careful is happening (cleaned windows, can't drive).

**The technique: find the detail that contains the period in its texture, not its labelling.** Don't say "it was 1943." Have characters navigate a world shaped by 1943 — rationing, the absence of men, war news on the wireless, the specific social disruptions.

#### Period Flavour vs Period Accuracy

**Period accuracy:** Events, technology, geography, vocabulary that are correct for the era.

**Period flavour:** The *feel* of the era — the moral atmosphere, the social textures, the way people's assumptions about the world differ from a contemporary reader's.

In crime fiction, **period flavour matters more than period accuracy**. A minor anachronism (a product that didn't exist quite yet, a slang term that's slightly off-period) is far less damaging than a failure of moral atmosphere — characters who think and feel like 21st-century people in 1940s clothing.

**The specific challenge of 1940s NZ:**
- **Wartime psychology:** Fear of invasion (NZ was genuinely under threat in 1942-43), absent men, women in non-traditional roles *during* the war but not after, the specific anxieties of isolation
- **Colonial social structure:** British identity still very strong; "Mother Country" discourse was real and heartfelt for many; Māori/Pākehā dynamics were dramatically different from contemporary NZ
- **Class:** More rigid than contemporary NZ, with British-inflected class markers (accent, education, religion)
- **Gender:** Women in more public roles during the war, actively rolled back post-war. 1945-1950 specifically is a period of reinscription of gender norms.
- **Violence:** A society that had just lived through years of war news — including men who had done terrible things and seen terrible things — had a different relationship to violence than pre-war NZ society.

#### Language — Period-Appropriate Vocabulary

**Principle:** No word that is unmistakably post-period. No slang that was coined later. No conceptual vocabulary that didn't exist.

**1940s NZ-specific considerations:**
- American idiom beginning to enter NZ speech (via American troops stationed in NZ during the war) — this is historically accurate and interesting
- British broadcast English as model for formal speech
- Māori words in the environment but typically not in Pākehā dialogue
- Radio rather than television shapes cultural reference
- Idioms of scarcity, of rationing, of war news

**The anachronism test:** Read each distinctive piece of dialogue or interiority and ask: could this have been said/thought in 1943? Not just the words — the underlying concept. Characters shouldn't think in terms of "trauma" (as a psychological concept), shouldn't conceptualise class or race the way post-1960s education would have taught them.

#### The Moral Landscape of the Period as Dramatic Tension

The most powerful thing historical crime fiction can do is use the moral assumptions of the period as *pressure*, not just background. In 1940s NZ:

- **Women who step out of prescribed roles** are subject to specific, documented social sanction. A female investigator (formal or informal) would face resistance that is structural, not merely the individual antagonism of specific sexist men.
- **Māori characters** exist in a specific social position that has real historical texture — post-war, pre-land march, in a city like Auckland, navigating a Pākehā world that had specific assumptions.
- **Returned soldiers** are dealing with what we now call PTSD without that vocabulary, without that legitimacy, without the social support systems. Violence close to the surface. Silence enforced by the culture.
- **Homosexuality** is criminal in 1940s NZ. A gay character in this period is already involved in concealment and risk — this is not a minor character texture, it is a fundamental plot pressure.

These are **dramatic resources**, not merely historical context. The moral landscape of the period should create specific, plot-relevant pressures.

### NEW ZEALAND CRIME FICTION

#### The Emerging Tradition

**Paul Thomas** (*Inside Dope*, the Ihaka series): Thomas pioneered serious NZ crime fiction. His detective Tito Ihaka is Māori — a deliberate choice to position the investigator inside the society, not outside it. Thomas uses the Māori perspective not as exotic colour but as a specific lens through which NZ's social hypocrisies are visible. Ihaka sees the Pākehā establishment from outside; his outsider-inside status creates the moral position that French-style detectives have through individual damage.

**Vanda Symon** (the Sam Shephard series): Set in Dunedin and Southland — the specific texture of small-city and rural NZ. What Symon captures: the small-community dynamic where everyone knows everyone, where investigation is also social navigation, where the investigator's *own community* is implicated. This is crucially different from the anonymous-city detective tradition: in small NZ communities, finding the killer means destroying someone's family, community, or your own standing.

**What makes NZ crime distinctive:**

- **Landscape of isolation.** The New Zealand bush and landscape is not neutral. It is old, specifically textured, and in crime fiction it carries death well — bodies don't get found in NZ bush. The landscape can conceal. It is also genuinely sublime in the original sense: beautiful, indifferent, overwhelming.
- **Small-community dynamics.** Everyone knows everyone, which means everyone knows everyone's secrets, which means investigation is also excavation of the community's self-protective silence. The community's **silence** is often the real antagonist.
- **Colonial tension.** The specific unresolved tension of Aotearoa NZ — land, language, history — is always present. It doesn't have to be foregrounded, but a NZ crime novel that is entirely unaware of it will feel false to NZ readers.
- **Isolation from the world.** 1940s NZ is genuinely at the end of the world — the war is happening elsewhere, news arrives by ship and radio, the cultural reference points are British by habit and American by proximity. This geographical isolation shapes psychology.

## DOMAIN 4: STRUCTURE — THE CRIME NOVEL

### THREE-ACT STRUCTURE IN CRIME FICTION

**Act One — Setup (roughly 0-25% of novel):**
- The world before the crime (establishes normal, makes us care about what will be disrupted)
- The inciting crime (or its discovery)
- The detective commits to the case (creates the forward question: will they solve it?)
- The detective's wound is established (shows what they bring to this case and what it will cost them)
- Stakes: personal, professional, moral

**Act Two — Confrontation (roughly 25-75% of novel):**
- Investigation: pursuit of leads, encounters with suspects, accumulation of evidence
- A series of defeats — the investigation repeatedly fails, reverses, hits walls
- The wound is activated by the case
- The midpoint reveal: approximately halfway, a revelation that reframes everything that's come before and points toward the new direction of the investigation
- False resolution (optional but effective, placed around 60-70%)
- The darkest moment: the detective has failed, the case appears unsolvable, the wound has done maximum damage

**Act Three — Resolution (roughly 75-100% of novel):**
- New insight/approach: the detective finds the frame that makes the evidence cohere
- Confrontation with the antagonist (usually)
- Resolution of the criminal case
- Resolution or partial resolution of the detective's wound
- The world after: what has changed, what has been lost, what has been learned

**The crime novel's specific three-act challenge:** The investigation arc (asking questions, gathering evidence, hitting dead ends) is inherently repetitive. The writer must ensure each cycle of investigation advances the *emotional* story as well as the informational one. Every scene must do something to the detective, not just to the case.

### THE INCITING CRIME — WHEN TO SHOW IT

**Option 1: Start before the crime.** The reader knows the victim before they die. This creates emotional investment in the solution — we're not just solving a puzzle; we're seeking justice for someone we knew. Risk: slowing the pace, possible disconnect if the victim section is too long.

**Option 2: Start with the discovery.** The most common choice — the investigator finds the body. Plunges immediately into investigation. Risk: the victim is an abstraction; the reader is solving a puzzle, not seeking justice.

**Option 3: Start after (prolepsis/flash forward).** Donna Tartt. Start with the murder known, the novel explaining how. Creates a different suspense — not whodunit but how-did-we-get-here and what-does-it-mean. Works best in literary crime where the *how* is the subject.

**Option 4: Start with the perpetrator.** The reader knows who did it; the question is whether the detective will find out. Inverted mystery (Columbo, Ruth Rendell's best work). Creates sympathy-management challenges: the reader is watching the crime and the cover-up simultaneously.

For 1940s NZ: the discovery structure works well because it allows the wartime social world to be established through the investigation, rather than requiring a pre-crime section to lay it in.

### THE INVESTIGATION ARC

**The problem:** Investigations are, by nature, episodic — lead, dead end, lead, dead end. Without craft intervention, this becomes repetitive.

**Solutions:**

1. **Each interview/lead reveals character as well as information.** The investigator meets a different segment of the social world with each scene; each scene advances our understanding of the world, not just the case.

2. **Personal cost accumulates.** Each scene in the investigation should cost the detective something. Energy, relationship, safety, self-knowledge. The investigation should be *wearing them down*.

3. **The case evolves.** The crime the detective thinks they're investigating in Act One is not the crime they're investigating in Act Three. Revelations should *change the nature of the question*, not just add information.

4. **Time pressure.** Even in historical fiction, there are time pressures: another body, a suspect about to leave, evidence about to be destroyed, the detective's own position becoming untenable.

5. **The investigator's method creates obstacles.** If the investigator has a specific method (intuitive/procedural/social), that method should have specific failure modes that the investigation hits.

### RED HERRINGS — PLANTING THEM FAIRLY

A red herring that is arbitrary or convenient is a cheat. A legitimate red herring:

- Points to a suspect who has *real* reason for suspicion (actual motive, actual opportunity, actual suspicious behaviour)
- The suspicious behaviour has a *real* explanation that is not the crime (they're hiding something, just not this)
- The false trail reveals something true about the world or the characters
- In retrospect, it is clear why the detective was misled — and the reader should be able to identify the moment where the correct reading was available but not taken

**The best red herrings serve double duty:** They mislead about the crime while revealing something true about the character, community, or theme. In 1940s NZ, a red herring about wartime profiteering might mislead about the murder while revealing something true about economic opportunism in the period.

### THE REVEAL — STRUCTURING SURPRISE AND INEVITABILITY

The paradox of the great reveal: it must surprise AND be inevitable in retrospect.

**The mechanism:** The reader is surprised because the correct interpretation of the evidence was *available but occluded*. The reader had the information. They didn't see it. The reveal shows them what they missed — and immediately, they see that it was always there.

**Structural requirements:**
- The solution must be present in the evidence the reader has seen
- The misdirection must be *motivated*, not arbitrary (the reader should be able to trace why they were misled)
- The solution must be the *simplest* explanation for all the evidence (Occam's razor — over-complex solutions feel contrived)
- Character revelation should accompany the factual revelation: the killer is who they are for *reasons*, and those reasons should be revealed at the same time as the facts

**The reveal scene mechanics:**
- Don't use the "detective explains everything" convention if you can help it. If you must use it, put it in *conflict* — the detective explaining against a resistant or dangerous audience.
- The reveal should cost the detective something personally — it should not be purely triumphant.
- What happens after the reveal matters. The criminal's response, the community's response, the detective's emotional landing — these are what transform the reveal from a solution into a *meaning*.

### OPENING PAGES — THE FIRST FIVE PAGES

What must happen:

1. **Voice establishes itself.** The reader knows what kind of book this is, who is telling it, and what register to read it in. By page 1.

2. **A question is raised.** An obligation to the reader. Something that makes the next page necessary. This is not necessarily the crime — it is the hook of curiosity or dread or desire to know more.

3. **A character is present enough to care about.** Not necessarily described — but doing or experiencing something specific enough that they are *a person*, not a placeholder.

4. **The world is established without being described.** The reader understands where and when they are through what the character does and notices, not through the author's description.

5. **The register is established.** Literary crime, pulp noir, procedural thriller, domestic suspense — each has a distinct register, and the first page must commit.

The first page test (what agents actually do): Read the first page. Does it create a *want* in the reader? Want to know what happens. Want to stay with this person. Want to inhabit this world. Want to find out. If not, the manuscript gets passed.

### FIRST LINES — TWENTY EXAMPLES ANALYSED

**"What I warn you to remember is that I am a detective."** *(Tana French, In the Woods)*
→ Warning + self-description + instability. Why warn us? What is he warning us about? The first-person narrator flags their own unreliability in the act of asserting authority.

**"It was the bright yellow tape that finally convinced me my sister was dead."** *(Hilary Davidson, The Damage Done)*
→ Delayed acceptance of death. "Finally" — implies prior denial. The specific detail (yellow tape) grounds it. The word "convinced" implies resistance. We're immediately in a psychology.

**"Eunice Parchman killed the Coverdale family because she could not read or write."** *(Ruth Rendell, A Judgement in Stone)*
→ The killer, the victims, and the motive in one sentence. Inverted mystery — we know whodunit. The motive is shocking in its pedestrian nature. Everything that follows is explanation of a sentence already given.

**"The last camel died at noon."** *(Ken Follett, The Key to Rebecca)*
→ Economy, precision, stakes. The camel's death implies a desert, a journey, survival stakes. "At noon" is specific. The world is hostile.

**"Some years later, on a tugboat in the Gulf of Mexico, Joe Coughlin's feet were placed in a tub of cement."** *(Dennis Lehane, Live By Night)*
→ Prolepsis (flash forward). We know Joe survives (or the story ends here). The specific image (tugboat, Gulf, cement) is film-noir perfect. "Some years later" establishes that the novel will cover time.

**"Death is my beat."** *(Michael Connelly, The Poet)*
→ Three words. Reporter's voice. Cop's world. Both at once. Declares the subject and the register.

**"When the phone rang, Parker was in the garage, killing a man."** *(Richard Stark, Firebreak)*
→ The mundane interrupts the extraordinary. The juxtaposition is the joke and the premise. Tone established immediately (cool, competent, amoral protagonist).

**"It is cold at six-forty in the morning on a March day in Paris, and seems even colder when a man is about to be executed by firing squad."** *(Frederick Forsyth, The Day of the Jackal)*
→ Specific time and place anchor us. "Seems even colder" introduces the observer whose observation we trust. The execution, held until the end of the sentence, lands with the weight of all the specificity before it.

**"We were about to give up and call it a night when somebody dropped the girl off the bridge."** *(John D. MacDonald, Darker Than Amber)*
→ Active past tense, first person plural (we — there's a community), the specific action (dropped) rather than the euphemism (threw), the girl — already a victim rather than a person. The scene is already in motion before the line ends.

**"Ten days after the war ended, my sister Laura drove a car off a bridge."** *(Margaret Atwood, The Blind Assassin)*
→ Timeline establishes the world (postwar), family relationship, ambiguous death (drove off? intentionally?). The precision of "ten days" suggests the narrator has thought about this too much.

**"It must have been Friday because the fish smell from the Mansion House coffee-shop next door was strong enough to build a garage on."** *(Raymond Chandler, Bay City Blues)*
→ Pure Chandler: the deduction from sense perception, the simile (strong enough to build a garage on), the specificity of the fish smell, the hardboiled voice. We know exactly who is narrating.

**"Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of the screenplay, I will fear no network executive."** *(Michael Tolkin)*
→ Shows how parody of a form can be a strong opening — here the religious parody announces the satirical register immediately.

**"Winter came like an anarchist with a bomb."** *(Ed McBain, The Pusher)*
→ Simile as political commentary. Violence in the weather. Announces a world of threat and instability.

**"I feel compelled to report that at the moment of death, my entire life did not pass before my eyes in a flash."** *(Sue Grafton, I is for Innocent)*
→ The narrator is dead — or was dead. Kinsey's dry, ironic voice established immediately. Undermines the cliché (death narrative clichés) in the very act of invoking it.

**"The night of my mother's funeral, Linda Dawson cried on my shoulder, put her tongue in my mouth and asked me to find her husband."** *(Declan Hughes, The Wrong Kind of Blood)*
→ Triple action in one sentence: grief, sexuality, investigation. All in incongruous juxtaposition. The Irish hardboiled voice established through cadence and the absurdity.

**"It was one hell of a night to throw away a baby."** *(Julia Spencer-Fleming, In the Bleak Midwinter)*
→ The casual brutality of "throw away" applied to a baby. The weather implied by "hell of a night." Moral horror in a single sentence.

**"When I finally caught up with Abraham Trahearne, he was drinking beer with an alcoholic bulldog named Fireball Roberts in a ramshackle joint just outside of Sonoma, California, drinking the heart right out of a fine spring afternoon."** *(James Crumley, The Last Good Kiss)*
→ One of the great crime novel opening sentences. The name (Abraham Trahearne — character before explanation). The alcoholic bulldog (specificity and absurdity). "Drinking the heart right out of a fine spring afternoon" — beautiful destruction as metaphor.

**"Bill Roberts decided to rob the firecracker stand on account he didn't have a job and not a nickel's worth of money and his mother was dead and kind of freeze-dried in her bedroom."** *(Joe Lansdale, Freezer Burn)*
→ The flat causality of the reasoning (therefore rob). The escalating awfulness of the reasons. "Freeze-dried" — darkly comic, specific, unforgettable.

**"The ambulance is still miles away when Dana awakens to the near dark of evening."** *(Susan Crawford, The Pocket Wife)*
→ Present tense, plunges in. The ambulance has been called; we don't know why. Character named but ungrounded. "Near dark of evening" has texture. We're inside a scene already.

**"Geneva Sweet ran an orange extension cord past Mayva Greenwood, Beloved Wife and Mother, May She Rest with Her Heavenly Father."** *(Attica Locke, Bluebird, Bluebird)*
→ The gravestone epitaph in the sentence. We're in a cemetery. The orange cord runs past a dead woman's epitaph — the living and the dead in the same sentence. The name is specific, the epitaph is specific, the image is precise and haunting.

## DOMAIN 5: LINE EDITING STANDARDS

### WHAT SEPARATES PUBLISHABLE FROM ALMOST-THERE

#### Errors That Mark Amateur Writing at the Line Level

**1. Telling what should be shown (but not all telling — the wrong telling):**
The amateur tells us *about* characters rather than showing them in action. Specifically: emotional conclusions (*"She was devastated"*) where the emotion should be rendered through behaviour and detail.

**2. Redundancy and over-explanation:**
- *"She nodded her head"* — you can't nod anything but your head. Cut: *"She nodded."*
- *"He stood up to his feet"* — you can't stand up any other way. Cut: *"He stood."*
- Explaining the implication of an action: *"She slammed the door, showing her anger."* The slamming showed it. The explanation undoes the show.
- Repeating in different words what was just demonstrated: *"He smiled. He was happy."*

**3. Adverb abuse:**
Adverbs on dialogue tags (*"she said angrily"*) are the most common form. If the dialogue doesn't convey the anger, the adverb is doing too much work; if the dialogue does convey it, the adverb is redundant.

The adverb problem: adverbs modify verbs, which means they're correcting weak verbs. *"He ran quickly"* → *"He sprinted."* The specific verb contains the adverb's information and does it with more precision.

**Appropriate adverb use:** When the adverb creates a meaningful surprise or complication relative to the verb. *"She smiled bitterly"* — the smile and the bitterness are in tension. This is different from *"She frowned unhappily"* — redundant.

**4. Passive constructions that bleed energy:**
*"The gun was picked up by Michael"* has no momentum. *"Michael picked up the gun"* has agency.

The passive voice is appropriate in specific circumstances:
- The agent is unknown (*"The safe had been opened"*)
- The agent is deliberately withheld (mystery technique)
- The tone is formal/official (documents within the fiction)
- The action done TO the subject is what matters

**5. Filter words and distance:**
Every "he saw," "she noticed," "he heard" puts the author between the reader and the experience. In moments of intensity, this is a fatal bleed of tension.

Compare:
*"He noticed there was blood on the doorknob."*
*"Blood on the doorknob."*

The second version is at the doorknob. The first is watching a character at the doorknob.

**6. Throat clearing:**
Sentences or paragraphs that approach the actual point without reaching it. The warm-up before the thing. 

*"It was a cold day, and Detective Morrison had been working since six, and he was tired in a way that went beyond tiredness, and when he looked at the file on his desk he wondered if he'd ever solve this case."*

The real sentence is: *"Morrison stared at the file and knew, with the particular clarity of exhaustion, that he was missing something."* Everything before is warm-up.

**7. Cliché:**
Phrases so worn they carry no weight:
- Knife/cold feeling through the chest
- World stopped / time stood still
- She didn't know how long she sat there
- A cold sweat
- Heart hammering / pounding
- The smell of blood and fear (fear doesn't smell)
- A dark and stormy night (any weather that "sets the mood")

**The cliché test:** If you've read the phrase before in another book, it's a candidate for replacement. If you've read it in ten books, it's a cliché.

**The replacement challenge:** Don't replace cliché with a forced, ornate alternative. Find the *specific* version of what you're trying to say. Not "fear gripped her heart" but: what specifically was she doing? What did the fear make her do?

**8. Hedging into meaninglessness:**
*"He seemed to almost kind of hesitate."* This sentence contains three separate hedges ("seemed to," "almost," "kind of") applied to a single action. Pick one or use none.

**9. Weak attributions for dialogue:**
*"He ejaculated"* (historical use but now unavoidable comedy). *"She breathed"* (you can't breathe dialogue). *"He hissed"* (you can't hiss a sentence without sibilants). 

The standard: *"said"* is invisible and correct. *"Asked"* is correct for questions. Anything beyond these should be used sparingly and only when the physical quality of the speech is information (whispered, shouted). Action beats are better than unusual attributions.

#### How a Professional Line Editor Thinks

The questions a line editor asks of every sentence:

1. **Is this sentence doing something?** Moving the plot, revealing character, building atmosphere, advancing theme. If it's doing none of these, it should be cut.

2. **Is it doing it as directly as possible?** Have I found the most efficient path between intention and execution?

3. **Is the verb doing the work?** Or is the verb weak and the meaning deferred to adverbs, adjectives, and subordinate clauses?

4. **Is this specific?** Or generic? "She picked up a cup" vs "She picked up his cup." The second is a choice, a claim. The first is furniture.

5. **Am I in the right head?** Is everything in this passage accessible to the POV character? Am I reporting or showing?

6. **Does the rhythm serve the moment?** Long accumulation for psychological weight, short sentences for action or impact?

7. **Does the sentence trust the reader?** Or is it explaining what the reader already knows?

8. **Is this a cliché?** Have I read this construction elsewhere?

9. **Is there anything here that shouldn't be here?** The "darlings" — the beautifully written passage that slows the pace, the clever observation that takes the reader out of the scene, the research displayed rather than deployed.

### STRUCTURAL EDITING

#### Assessing Whether a Scene Earns Its Place

Every scene must justify its existence by meeting at least one of these criteria:
1. Advances the plot (causes or resolves conflict, reveals information)
2. Reveals character in a way that can't be achieved elsewhere
3. Creates or intensifies tension required for a later scene
4. Establishes something about the world that matters for the story

The "cut test": If you cut this scene, what is lost? If the honest answer is "nothing essential," the scene should be cut or merged with another.

**The most common scene that fails:** The scene that exists to demonstrate research, or to show a place or relationship the author enjoys, without advancing any of the above.

**The scene that seems to fail but doesn't:** The quiet scene that builds dread, that establishes a relationship that will be shattered, that gives the reader a breath before the next confrontation. This scene earns its place through *contrast effect* — it is necessary for the scenes around it, even if it doesn't advance plot on its own.

#### Pacing Problems

**Scenes that drag:**
- Too much interiority at the expense of action
- The character processes their feelings at length before acting
- Multiple similar beats (three conversations that reveal the same information)
- Descriptions that aren't doing double work
- Too much consequence/sequel after a scene's disaster (the character thinks about it for five pages rather than moving)

**Scenes that rush:**
- Insufficient grounding — we don't know where we are or who's present
- Action without consequence — things happen but we don't register their emotional impact
- Dialogue without context — we understand the words but not why they matter
- The reveal handled so quickly the reader doesn't have time to feel it

**The pacing test:** Read the problem section aloud. The voice will tell you where the energy drops. Where you rush your reading, the prose is dragging. Where you slow down and lean in, the prose is working.

#### The Darlings Problem

The "kill your darlings" instruction (attributed to Faulkner, Chekhov, and others) specifically targets: the writing you're most proud of that isn't serving the story. Typically: a beautiful passage that the author loves for its beauty, which stops the story dead.

**How to identify a darling:**
- You know exactly where it is (you've been hoping no one will ask you to cut it)
- The surrounding narrative would flow more naturally without it
- It's longer than the scene requires
- It demonstrates something about the author's skill rather than revealing something about the character/story

**The test:** Cover it with your hand. Read the passage before and the passage after. Does the second flow naturally from the first? If yes, the darling is probably cuttable.

#### POV Drift

POV drift is the slow slippage from close POV toward omniscient narration. It happens when:
- The author adds information the POV character couldn't have
- The author describes the POV character's external appearance (she didn't know how she looked; only an external viewer would)
- The narrative tone becomes more literary/authoritative than the POV character's consciousness would be
- Action beats between dialogue are written as if observed from outside rather than experienced from inside

**The fix:** For every piece of information, ask: how does the POV character know this? If there's no good answer, the information is coming from somewhere outside the POV character — which means you've drifted.

#### Dialogue Tags and Action Beats

**The standard:**
- Dialogue tags: *said* is invisible. *Asked* for questions. Everything else is a choice that should be made deliberately and sparingly.
- Action beats instead of tags: *"'This is wrong.' He set the cup down."* — the action beat tells us who spoke, how they're feeling, what they're doing. Richer than a tag.
- Action beats should be in the same paragraph as the dialogue they attach to, not separated.
- Punctuation standard: *"This is dialogue," she said.* / *"Is this dialogue?" he asked.* / *"This is dialogue." She set it down.* (Full stop, not comma, before an action beat that is a full sentence)

## DOMAIN 6: THE PUBLISHING STANDARD

### WHAT AGENTS AND EDITORS ACTUALLY WANT

#### The First Page Test

What an agent does with the first page:
- Reads for *voice* first — is there a distinctive, controlled, engaging voice? If yes, they read on. If no, they stop.
- Reads for *promise* — does this opening promise something worth 300 pages? Crime fiction must promise: a compelling investigation, a detective worth following, a world worth inhabiting.
- Reads for *competence* — are there immediate technical problems (POV issues, cliché, over-writing, under-writing)?

The first page must answer: **Who is this? Where are they? Why does this matter?** Not necessarily through exposition — through *rendering*.

#### What's Selling and What's Saturated

**Currently strong in crime/thriller (2024-2026):**
- Psychological suspense with female protagonists (domestic noir remains strong but market is beginning to saturate)
- Historical crime with specific, less-explored settings (1940s NZ is differentiated — not Victorian London or WWII Paris)
- "Literary crime" — elevated prose, character-driven, permeable borders with literary fiction (Tana French's legacy)
- Social/political crime (crime as lens on systemic injustice)
- Cold case investigations with contemporary resonance

**Oversaturated:**
- "Girl" novels: *The Girl on the Train* derivatives
- Gone Girl structure without the craft to sustain it
- Serial killer procedurals (unless genuinely distinguished)
- Amateur sleuth cozies (except in specifically developing markets)

**Where 1940s NZ fits:**
The market for historical crime with non-European, non-American settings is good and growing. NZ specifically is underrepresented — this is an advantage. Comparable titles would be drawn from: post-war European crime (Donna Leon's Venice, Andrea Camilleri's Sicily), colonial-era crime (Alexander McCall Smith's Botswana, though different tone), Antipodean crime fiction (Kerry Greenwood's Phryne Fisher, set in Melbourne 1920s — probably the closest comp).

#### Comparable Titles

For a 1940s NZ crime novel, comp titles would likely include:
- Kerry Greenwood (*Cocaine Blues*, *Miss Fisher's Murder Mysteries*) — 1920s Melbourne: period, Antipodean, female investigator
- Alan Bradley (Flavia de Luce series) — period mystery, literary crime
- Vanda Symon or Paul Thomas — contemporary NZ crime, for NZ-market positioning
- Tana French — for literary crime readers who will appreciate elevated prose

The comp title conversation is about market positioning: you're telling the agent *who will buy this book* and *where it will sit in a bookshop*. Historical + NZ setting + literary crime register is a specific, identifiable market position.

#### Query Letter Craft

The query for crime fiction should:
1. Open with the hook — the central tension of the story in one or two sentences
2. Identify the protagonist and their specific wound/stake
3. State the crime/investigation and why it matters
4. Identify the specific obstacle (not "the investigation is dangerous" — what specific danger, what specific stake?)
5. Comp titles and word count
6. Brief author bio

The 1940s NZ setting is a *selling point*, not an obstacle. It should be in the hook, not buried. Agents are looking for differentiated settings.

## THE EDITORIAL EYE — A SYNTHESISED FRAMEWORK

*How to read and edit a crime manuscript at publishable standard.*

### THE METHODOLOGY: THREE PASSES

Professional manuscript editing is not one read — it is at minimum three, each at a different scale. Don't try to do all three at once.

---

### PASS ONE: STRUCTURAL READING

**Scale:** Whole novel. Chapter summaries. Macro-level.

**Reading mode:** Fast, with notes but without line-editing impulses. You are reading as a story.

**Questions to answer:**

**About the crime:**
- Is the crime compelling enough to carry 300+ pages?
- Is it clear when the reader first understands what crime they're investigating?
- Does the crime connect to the world of the novel — is it *of this place and time* or could it happen anywhere?

**About the detective/protagonist:**
- What is the wound? Is it established early and present throughout?
- Does the wound connect to the crime? (It should)
- Does the wound cause specific failures? (It must)
- Does the protagonist have a genuine character arc — are they changed or further damaged by the investigation?

**About the investigation:**
- Does each investigation cycle advance the story (not just add information)?
- Is there genuine reversal and obstacle — or does the investigation proceed too smoothly?
- Is there a midpoint crisis that reframes the investigation?
- Is there a false resolution?
- Is there a dark night — the detective's lowest point?

**About the reveal:**
- Is it surprising AND inevitable?
- Was the evidence present?
- Does the reveal change the meaning of what we've read — or does it just add new information?

**About structure:**
- Where are the act breaks? Do they fall at the right places?
- Which scenes can be cut? Which are missing?
- Are there any scenes that repeat the same function?

**Red flags at structural level:**
- Investigation that solves too easily
- Detective who learns nothing
- Reveal that requires information not in the book
- Subplots that don't connect to the main theme
- The first 50 pages: does the story begin at the right place?

---

### PASS TWO: SCENE-LEVEL READING

**Scale:** Scene by scene. Chapter by chapter.

**Reading mode:** Slower. You are reading as an editor now, not as a story reader.

**Questions for each scene:**

1. **Goal:** What does the POV character want? Is it specific and immediate?
2. **Conflict:** Is there genuine opposition? Or does the scene proceed without resistance?
3. **Outcome:** Does the scene end in a disaster (worsened situation, new problem, failed goal)?
4. **Closing beat:** Does the scene end with a hook — a question, a decision, a revelation that drives the reader forward?

**POV questions:**
- Is the POV consistent throughout the scene?
- Is everything in the scene accessible to the POV character?
- Is the scene rendered (shown through the POV's experience) or reported (told about from a distance)?

**Pacing questions:**
- Is the scene the right length? (Too long: scenes that repeat beats or process too much. Too short: scenes that rush through major revelations)
- Is the sequel (reaction/dilemma/decision after the scene) appropriately sized?

**Tension questions:**
- Does the scene have a clear stake?
- Does the tension build or remain flat?
- Is there a moment of genuine unpredictability?

**Red flags at scene level:**
- Scenes without conflict (people meeting, exchanging information pleasantly)
- Scenes that begin too early (arriving, setting up)
- Scenes that end too late (leaving, summarising what just happened)
- POV drift
- Major revelations handled so quickly they don't land
- Two consecutive scenes with the same function

---

### PASS THREE: LINE-LEVEL READING

**Scale:** Sentence by sentence. Word by word.

**Reading mode:** Slow, with pen. You are reading as a line editor.

**Questions for each sentence:**

1. **Is it doing something?** Does it advance plot, reveal character, build atmosphere, advance theme?
2. **Is the verb doing the work?** Specific, active, precise?
3. **Is this specific?** Or generic?
4. **Is this in POV?** Or has the author intruded?
5. **Filter words?** He saw/she noticed/he heard — can these be removed?
6. **Rhythm:** Does the sentence rhythm serve the moment?
7. **Cliché:** Have I read this construction before?
8. **Redundancy:** Is the same information given twice?
9. **Hedging:** Does the sentence commit to what it's saying?
10. **Throat clearing:** Does the sentence get to the point?

**Line-level red flags to mark systematically:**
- Filter words (highlight in one colour)
- Adverbs on dialogue tags (highlight)
- Passive constructions (highlight)
- Clichés (highlight)
- "Was/had been/started to/began to/seemed to" (flag but don't automatically cut — context matters)
- Redundancy (double marks)
- Paragraphs longer than one page (flag for possible break or trim)

**The sentence-rhythm pass:**
Read the passage aloud. Where you stumble, there's a rhythm problem. Where the voice drops, there's an energy problem. Where you rush through, there's either excellent momentum or skimming-through-boredom — ask which.

---

### APPLYING THE FRAMEWORK TO 1940S NZ CRIME

**Specific questions for this material:**

**Period authenticity check:**
- Does every piece of dialogue pass the period test? (Could this have been said in 1943?)
- Are the characters' assumptions and worldview period-appropriate?
- Does the period create specific, plot-relevant pressure — or is it merely costume?
- Is the landscape *felt* rather than described?

**NZ-specific questions:**
- Is there a sense of the specific quality of NZ isolation — geographic, cultural, emotional?
- Are the class and ethnic dynamics of 1940s NZ present and accurate?
- Does the small-community dynamic shape the investigation?
- Is the landscape doing character/mood work, not just establishing where we are?

**The detective's wound in period context:**
What specific wound makes sense for a 1940s NZ context?
- Returned soldier's unprocessed violence
- Woman operating in a male-dominated world
- Someone whose community involvement creates specific blind spots (can't see evil in people they grew up with)
- Someone from outside (immigrant, Māori in a Pākehā space, or vice versa) whose outsider status provides the moral clarity of the detective

**Thematic question:**
What is the novel *about* — not what happens in it, but what it is an investigation of? The best crime novels investigate a moral question through the mechanism of criminal investigation. What is the specific moral question this 1940s NZ novel is asking?
