## 1. THE BRAIN'S ARCHITECTURE (What drives everything)

### The Triune Brain Model (simplified but useful)
- **Reptilian brain (brainstem/cerebellum):** Survival — breathing, heart rate, basic threat detection
- **Limbic brain (amygdala, hippocampus):** Emotion, memory, social bonding, reward
- **Neocortex (prefrontal cortex):** Reasoning, planning, impulse control, nuance

Key insight: **These systems don't operate democratically.** The limbic system can override the neocortex. That's why "knowing better" isn't enough — you have to work with the emotional brain, not just the rational one.

### Amygdala-Prefrontal Dynamic (LeDoux)
- Amygdala fires *before* conscious awareness (~200ms)
- The amygdala→PFC pathway has more connections than PFC→amygdala
- Under high emotional load, the PFC gets suppressed — nuance collapses
- This is the neurological basis of: rage, splitting, tunnel vision, poor decisions under stress
- **Practical implication:** You can't reason someone (or yourself) out of a hijacked state. You have to regulate first, reason second.

### Polyvagal Theory (Porges)
Three nervous system states:
1. **Ventral vagal (safe):** Open, curious, connected, creative, regulated
2. **Sympathetic (mobilised):** Fight or flight — activated, anxious, reactive
3. **Dorsal vagal (shutdown):** Freeze, collapse, dissociation, depression

Most behavioural problems are nervous system dysregulation, not character flaws.
**Regulation tools:** Exercise, breathwork, cold exposure, prayer/meditation, safe social connection, rhythm (music, walking)

## 2. PERSONALITY (The stable stuff)

### Big Five (OCEAN) — Most research-validated model
- **Openness:** Curiosity, creativity, novelty-seeking. Linked to dopamine activity.
- **Conscientiousness:** Self-discipline, goal pursuit. Best predictor of life outcomes.
- **Extraversion:** Social energy, dominance-seeking, positive affect. Linked to dopamine sensitivity.
- **Agreeableness:** Cooperation, empathy, conflict avoidance. Linked to serotonin/oxytocin.
- **Neuroticism:** Emotional reactivity, negative affect sensitivity. Linked to threat/amygdala reactivity.

Research (PMC): Big Five traits correlate with specific brain region volumes. Not fixed — traits can shift with deliberate effort, therapy, major life events.

### Dark Triad (when useful to understand)
- **Narcissism:** Grandiosity + fragile self-esteem. Driven by need for admiration. Research (biorxiv 2024): linked to amygdala-PFC-ACC connectivity patterns.
- **Machiavellianism:** Strategic, detached, low empathy in pursuit of goals
- **Psychopathy:** Low fear response, high impulsivity, reduced empathy circuitry

## 3. MOTIVATION (Why people do what they do)

### Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) — Most robust motivation framework
Three basic psychological needs:
1. **Autonomy** — feeling self-directed, not controlled
2. **Competence** — feeling capable and effective
3. **Relatedness** — feeling connected to others

When these three needs are met → intrinsic motivation, persistence, wellbeing.
When frustrated → motivation collapses, resentment, disengagement.

**Application:** If someone (or you) loses motivation, check which need is being blocked. Usually one of these three.

### Motivation types (SDT spectrum)
- Amotivation → External regulation → Introjected → Identified → Integrated → Intrinsic
- The further right on this scale, the more sustainable and fulfilling the motivation
- Guilt, shame, and external pressure are left-side motivators — work short-term, burn out

### Goal types (Research: goals + emotions are dynamically linked)
- **Approach goals** (move toward something desirable) → positive affect, engagement
- **Avoidance goals** (escape something bad) → anxiety, vigilance, depletion
- Best outcomes: approach goals driven by intrinsic motivation

## 4. HABIT FORMATION (How behaviour becomes automatic)

### The Habit Loop (Duhigg / Graybiel)
**Cue → Routine → Reward**
- Encoded in the **basal ganglia** (striatum)
- Once encoded, becomes automatic — brain stops conscious decision-making
- This is why habits are hard to break: the neural pathway doesn't disappear, it competes

### Neuroscience
- **Dopamine** doesn't signal pleasure — it signals *prediction of reward* (incentive salience)
- The brain learns cues that predict reward and increases dopamine for the cue, not the reward itself
- This is why addiction is so powerful: the cue triggers dopamine even when the reward no longer satisfies

### Habit change mechanics
- Can't delete a habit — must replace the routine (keep cue + reward, swap routine)
- Implementation intentions work: "When X happens, I will do Y" (studies: 2-3x habit follow-through)
- Environment design > willpower: change the cue, not just the intention
- **66 days average** to automatise a new behaviour (Phillippa Lally, UCL) — not 21 days

## 5. EMOTION REGULATION

### Strategies (research-backed rankings)
**Adaptive:**
- Cognitive reappraisal (reframe the meaning) — most effective long-term
- Problem-solving
- Acceptance
- Mindfulness

**Maladaptive (short-term relief, long-term amplification):**
- Rumination (replaying events) — most strongly linked to depression/anxiety
- Suppression (pushing down feelings) — increases physiological arousal
- Catastrophising
- Self-blame

Key research finding (Alawadhi et al 2024): People with anxiety/depression use BOTH adaptive and maladaptive strategies MORE than others — they're working overtime emotionally. The maladaptive ones dominate.

### The emotion-behaviour gap
- The space between trigger and response is where all growth happens
- Naming emotions ("affect labelling") activates PFC, reduces amygdala reactivity (Lieberman, UCLA)
- Literal tip: saying "I notice I'm feeling angry" out loud is a neurological intervention

## 6. THE SHADOW & PROJECTION (Jung — still the best model)

### Core concept
The shadow = the parts of self that have been rejected, suppressed, or deemed unacceptable.
- Not inherently "dark" — includes positive traits suppressed out of shame or social conditioning
- The more harshly a trait was judged in childhood, the deeper it's buried

### Projection mechanism
- We don't see our shadow directly — we see it in others
- The stronger the emotional reaction to a trait in someone else, the higher the likelihood that trait is in our own shadow
- Triggers are maps, not accidents

### Integration (the work)
- Not about accepting bad behaviour — about owning the *energy* behind it
- "Integrating the shadow" means: I don't have to act arrogantly, but I can own my need to be seen, heard, dominant — and channel it constructively

## 7. ATTACHMENT THEORY (How early wiring shapes adult behaviour)

### Four styles (Bowlby/Ainsworth/Main)
1. **Secure:** Trust, comfort with intimacy and independence
2. **Anxious/preoccupied:** Fear of abandonment, hypervigilance to social threat, needs reassurance
3. **Avoidant/dismissive:** Suppresses needs, discomfort with intimacy, values self-sufficiency
4. **Disorganised/fearful:** Both craves and fears closeness — often trauma background

### Neuroscience link
- Attachment patterns shape nervous system baseline (Polyvagal connection)
- Insecure attachment → **hypervigilance** — brain scanning social environment for threat constantly
- This is metabolically expensive and explains chronic fatigue, irritability, difficulty relaxing

### Adult relationships
- People unconsciously recreate attachment patterns from childhood
- The person who "triggers" you most is often activating an attachment wound
- Can change — therapy (especially EMDR, somatic, IFS) + consistently safe relationships rewire the pattern

## 8. ADDICTION & RECOVERY NEUROSCIENCE

### Three-stage cycle (Koob, NIDA)
1. **Binge/intoxication:** Basal ganglia, dopamine surge, reward encoding
2. **Withdrawal/negative affect:** Extended amygdala activates — dysphoria, anxiety, stress. Baseline pleasure drops.
3. **Preoccupation/anticipation:** PFC hijacked — executive control diminished, craving takes over

### What recovery actually does neurologically
- Restores PFC dominance over impulsive limbic circuits
- Rebuilds baseline dopamine tone (takes 6-18 months)
- Reduces amygdala hyperreactivity over time
- Nature 2024: emotional dysregulation is a *critical driver of relapse* — addressing negative emotions is as important as addressing cravings

### Why AA works (neuroscience perspective)
- Community → oxytocin, safe attachment, nervous system regulation
- Narrative (sharing) → processes trauma through verbalization, integrates limbic + cortical systems
- Structure/rituals → basal ganglia encoding, predictability reduces amygdala threat
- Steps as cognitive reappraisal exercises → reshape the meaning of past events
- Higher power concept → reduces hyper-ego, distributes the cognitive load of self-regulation

## 9. SOCIAL DYNAMICS & JUDGEMENT

### Why we judge harshly
- **Status threat:** The primal brain interprets confident dominance as a threat to our social standing
- **Social comparison:** Automatic — we constantly benchmark ourselves (PMC: produces negative emotional consequences when outcomes feel inferior)
- **Projection:** See #6 above
- **Identity protection:** Harsh judgement of "out-group" members reinforces in-group identity

### The mirror neuron piece
- fMRI studies: perspective-taking activates mirror neuron networks and *dampens moral outrage*
- The moment you genuinely imagine someone's inner world, they become human again
- This is why AA "identify, don't compare" is powerful — it hijacks the judgement circuit

## 10. WELLBEING — What actually works (meta-analysis consensus)

Top evidence-based contributors to sustained wellbeing:
1. **Physical exercise** — most consistent effect size across all studies. Antidepressant equivalent.
2. **Social connection** — quality, not quantity. One deep relationship > many shallow ones.
3. **Purpose/meaning** — not happiness as a goal, but engagement with meaningful activity
4. **Sleep** — foundational. Everything degrades without it.
5. **Nature exposure** — measurable cortisol reduction, attentional restoration
6. **Gratitude practice** — retrains the brain's negativity bias
7. **Contribution/service** — giving activates reward circuits more durably than receiving

What doesn't work as expected:
- Money (plateaus fast beyond comfort)
- Achievement alone (hedonic adaptation — returns to baseline)
- Trying to be happy directly (paradox of effort)

## 11. COGNITIVE BIASES & DECISION MAKING (Kahneman / Tversky)

### System 1 vs System 2 (Dual Process Theory)
- **System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, pattern-matching, effortless
- **System 2:** Slow, deliberate, logical, effortful, energy-expensive
- Most decisions are System 1 wearing a System 2 costume (we think we're reasoning when we're rationalising)

### Most impactful cognitive biases
- **Confirmation bias:** Seek/interpret info that confirms existing beliefs. Most pervasive of all biases.
- **Sunk cost fallacy:** Keep investing in something because of past investment, not future value
- **Availability heuristic:** Judge probability by how easily examples come to mind (recent/vivid = seems common)
- **Fundamental attribution error:** Attribute others' behaviour to character; own behaviour to circumstances
- **Dunning-Kruger effect:** Low competence → overconfidence; growing competence → growing doubt
- **Negativity bias:** Negative events weighted ~3x more heavily than equivalent positive ones (evolutionary)
- **Anchoring:** First number/piece of info heard disproportionately influences all subsequent judgements
- **In-group bias:** Automatically favour people perceived as "like us"
- **Hindsight bias:** "I knew it all along" — history rewritten after outcome is known
- **Optimism bias:** Overestimate positive outcomes for self, underestimate negative ones

### Debiasing — what actually works
- Slowing down (System 2 engagement)
- Pre-mortem thinking: "Imagine this failed — why?"
- Seeking disconfirming evidence deliberately
- Steel-manning opposing views before dismissing them
- Structured decision frameworks (removes emotional hijack)

## 12. CBT — COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS (Beck)

The 13 core distortions from Aaron Beck's model (most evidence-based therapy framework):

1. **All-or-nothing thinking (splitting)** — "If it's not perfect, it's a failure"
2. **Catastrophising** — Assuming worst possible outcome
3. **Mind reading** — Assuming you know what others think
4. **Fortune telling** — Predicting negative outcomes as fact
5. **Emotional reasoning** — "I feel it, therefore it's true"
6. **Should statements** — Rigid rules that generate guilt/resentment
7. **Labelling** — "I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake"
8. **Personalisation** — Blaming self for things outside your control
9. **Mental filter** — Fixating on one negative while ignoring positives
10. **Discounting the positive** — "That doesn't count"
11. **Overgeneralisation** — "This always happens to me"
12. **Magnification/minimisation** — Inflating negatives, shrinking positives
13. **Jumping to conclusions** — Negative interpretations without evidence

**Key insight:** These aren't irrational per se — they're fast-path shortcuts that worked in some context and got over-applied. The work is catching them, naming them, and choosing a more accurate interpretation.

## 13. TRAUMA PSYCHOLOGY (van der Kolk / Levine / Porges)

### What trauma actually is
Trauma is not the event — it's what happens in the nervous system when the event overwhelms its capacity to process and integrate. Two people can experience the same event; one is traumatised, one isn't. It's about the nervous system's response, not the severity of the trigger.

### Types
- **Acute trauma:** Single event (accident, assault, loss)
- **Complex trauma (C-PTSD):** Repeated, often relational (childhood neglect, abuse, unstable caregiving)
- **Developmental trauma:** Trauma in early childhood that shapes the nervous system's baseline architecture

### How it lives in the body (van der Kolk — "The Body Keeps the Score")
- Trauma encodes as sensory memory, not narrative memory — which is why talk therapy alone often doesn't reach it
- The body holds tension, posture patterns, hypervigilance, and somatic symptoms as unprocessed trauma
- Triggers activate the same physiological state as the original event — the body doesn't distinguish past from present

### Complex trauma markers
- Hypervigilance (constant threat scanning)
- Difficulty trusting
- Emotional dysregulation or numbness
- Shame as a core identity (not "I did something bad" but "I am bad")
- Dissociation under stress
- Insomnia / disrupted sleep (nervous system stays on alert)

### Effective treatments (evidence-ranked)
1. **EMDR** — Strongest evidence for PTSD. Bilateral stimulation processes stuck memories
2. **Somatic Experiencing (Levine)** — Body-based, discharges stored autonomic activation. PMC: positive effects on PTSD symptoms
3. **IFS (Internal Family Systems)** — Parts-based model. Growing evidence base, particularly for complex trauma. Still emerging research.
4. **CBT/trauma-focused CBT** — Strong evidence for standard PTSD, less for complex
5. **Medication** — Useful adjunct, doesn't resolve underlying patterns

## 14. INTERNAL FAMILY SYSTEMS (IFS) — Richard Schwartz

### Core model
The psyche is not a single unit — it's a system of "parts." Different parts have different ages, fears, roles, and agendas. Most psychological suffering comes from parts being in conflict or extreme states.

### Three categories of parts
- **Managers:** Proactive protectors — control, perfectionism, planning, people-pleasing. Keep the system functional.
- **Firefighters:** Reactive protectors — activated when pain breaks through. Addictions, rage, numbing, dissociation. Not trying to hurt you — trying to put out the fire.
- **Exiles:** The wounded, vulnerable parts — usually formed in childhood. Carry pain, shame, fear. Managers and firefighters exist to keep exiles locked away.

### The Self
- Beyond parts is the "Self" — calm, curious, compassionate, confident, connected. Not a part — it's the core.
- Healing = Self leading the system, not a part running the show
- The goal isn't to eliminate parts — it's to unburden them and let them take less extreme roles

### Research status
- Growing evidence base — pilot studies show effectiveness for PTSD, depression
- Tandfonline 2025 scoping review: peer-reviewed research not yet consolidated but promising
- Widely used clinically despite evidence lag

## 16. MEN'S PSYCHOLOGY

### The help-seeking paradox
PMC systematic review 2024: Men avoid mental health support due to:
- Fear of being seen as weak
- Masculine identity conflict with vulnerability
- Social isolation when admitting struggle
- "Toughing it out" as identity-level value

Result: Men suffer longer, more severely, and die by suicide at 3-4x the rate of women.

**The counter-evidence:** Men who do engage with support (therapy, AA, mentorship, close friendships) show equally strong or stronger recovery outcomes than women. The barrier is entry, not capacity.

### What actually works for men
- Shared activity over "processing" (side-by-side > face-to-face)
- Purpose and competence as entry points (fix a problem → build trust → deeper conversation)
- Framing help as strategy not weakness ("optimising performance" vs "I'm struggling")
- Male peer communities (AA, sport, trades, faith communities) — normative for vulnerability within a frame

### Healthy masculine identity (integrated view)
Not "toxic" vs "soft" — both extremes are avoidance. Healthy masculinity includes:
- Strength in service of others, not dominance for its own sake
- Vulnerability as courage, not weakness
- Accountability without self-destruction
- Protecting and providing as values, not identity cages
- Knowing when to lead and when to follow

## 17. STOICISM & CBT — THE ANCIENT-MODERN CONNECTION

### The parallel (Robertson — "Philosophy of CBT")
Stoicism (particularly Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Seneca) is essentially CBT 2000 years early.

**Stoic dichotomy of control → CBT's cognitive restructuring:**
- "Some things are in our control, others are not" (Epictetus) = identifying what you can and cannot change
- CBT: challenge distortions about what you're responsible for

**Stoic negative visualisation → Exposure therapy / decatastrophising:**
- Imagine the worst that could happen → reduces fear of it → builds resilience
- Prevents hedonic adaptation (taking good things for granted)

**Stoic daily review → CBT thought records:**
- Marcus Aurelius' journaling = structured reflection on thoughts and actions
- Identifying where you acted in accordance with your values, where you didn't

**PMC 2023:** Multiple papers confirm Stoic philosophy has direct parallels with CBT interventions. Stoicism is increasingly being integrated into clinical practice.

### Key Stoic principles (actionable)
- **The obstacle is the way** (Marcus Aurelius): difficulty is the material for growth
- **You can't control events, only your response:** The Stoic core. Matches polyvagal regulation theory.
- **Memento mori:** Remembering death is not morbid — it clarifies priorities
- **Amor fati:** Love your fate. Not resignation — active embrace of what is

## 18. INFLUENCE & SOCIAL DYNAMICS (Cialdini)

### 7 Principles of Persuasion
1. **Reciprocity** — We feel obligated to return favours. First-givers have disproportionate influence.
2. **Commitment & Consistency** — Once we commit publicly, we act to stay consistent with that commitment
3. **Social proof** — "Others like me are doing it" — especially powerful under uncertainty
4. **Authority** — We defer to perceived experts and credentials
5. **Liking** — We're influenced by people we like (similarity, compliments, familiarity)
6. **Scarcity** — We value things more when they're rare or time-limited
7. **Unity** — We're influenced by those we perceive as "our tribe" (identity-based belonging)

### Application to sales (Lead Flow)
- **Reciprocity:** Give value first (free audit, helpful advice) before the pitch
- **Social proof:** Case studies, testimonials, "other pest control businesses in NZ are using this"
- **Authority:** Credentials, specificity, confidence in delivery
- **Liking:** Build genuine rapport, find common ground, use their language
- **Scarcity/urgency:** Limited onboarding slots, setup timeline

## 19. SLEEP & COGNITION (Critical for Glenn)

### What sleep deprivation does to the brain (PMC research)
- **Amygdala hyperreactivity:** Sleep loss increases amygdala reactivity to negative stimuli by ~60%
- **PFC disconnection:** Loss of mPFC-amygdala connectivity = loss of emotional regulation
- **Attentional lapses:** Sustained attention degrades fastest of all cognitive functions
- **Decision quality:** Risk assessment, impulse control, and planning all degrade significantly
- **Emotional bias:** Negative stimuli weighted more heavily; positive stimuli less registered

**The emotional equation:** Poor sleep + emotional trigger = much larger reaction than either alone. Sleep is not optional for emotional regulation — it IS emotional regulation.

### Zopiclone (relevant to Glenn's situation)
- Zopiclone is a Z-drug (non-benzodiazepine) — works on GABA receptors similarly to benzos
- Research shows it reduces sleep latency but **alters sleep architecture** — suppresses deep slow-wave sleep and REM
- REM sleep is critical for emotional memory processing and emotional regulation
- Long-term use (months/years) can create **rebound insomnia** and psychological dependence
- The 7mg dose is standard therapeutic; dependency is a well-documented risk at this dose with long-term use
- Not judgment — just: the sleep you're getting may not be doing the full cognitive/emotional restoration work

## 20. MEANING & PURPOSE (Frankl / positive psychology)

### Viktor Frankl — Logotherapy
- Humans' primary drive is not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but *meaning*
- Even in extreme suffering, meaning transforms the experience
- "He who has a why can bear almost any how" (Nietzsche, used by Frankl)
- Three sources of meaning: **Creating** (work, building), **Experiencing** (love, beauty, truth), **Suffering** (choosing your attitude toward unavoidable pain)

### Ikigai (Japanese concept — research-backed)
The intersection of:
- What you **love**
- What you're **good at**
- What the **world needs**
- What you can be **paid for**

When all four overlap: ikigai — reason for being. Each overlap without the others creates something, but not fulfilment.

### What the research says about purpose
- Having a clear sense of purpose reduces all-cause mortality by ~15% (multiple longitudinal studies)
- Purpose buffers against depression, anxiety, addiction relapse
- Purpose is not found — it's built, through action and reflection over time
