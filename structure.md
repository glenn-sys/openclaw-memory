# The Weight of Men — Proposed Chapter Structure
*Last updated: March 2026*

---

## Core Architecture

**Spine:** The deathbed scene (Noel, Gary, Glenn in the hospice room, Waihi) is the axis of the entire book. Everything before it is cause. Everything after is consequence. It sits at the structural midpoint — Chapter 11.

**Three voices:**
- Noel — close-third, cold, flat, equable. Never inside his head. Felt only through effect.
- Gary — close-third, slightly more interior. Self-deception visible from inside.
- Glenn — first person, confessional, exact. The narrator reconstructing.

**Alcoholism thread (runs through all three parts):**
- Shetland Islands grandfather: the origin — chronic alcoholic, petty thief, migrant. The crack in the foundation.
- Noel: functional, violent, the era that didn't name it. Built himself in opposition to his father — and still passed it on.
- Gary: maintenance/top-up, managed, hidden — then collapsed
- Glenn: escalating, nearly fatal on first real exposure — the accumulated weight of four generations

## Key Structural Decisions

1. **The Weight of Air sits at Ch 11 (midpoint).** The deathbed earns its power from everything before it. Played too early, before the reader knows these men, it lands at ~30% of its potential force.

2. **The Dark Room is one chapter.** The teenage orbit into drugs builds directly to the confrontation — Glenn drunk, asking Gary *why don't you love me?* One arc, one chapter. The environment produces the moment.

3. **Gary needs more weight in Part Two.** He's the transmission mechanism between Noel and Glenn. The affairs, the Jeremy scene, the dark room confrontation are his defining scenes.

4. **The alcoholism progression must be concrete.** Each man needs at least one scene where his relationship with alcohol is *shown*, not summarised. The escalation Shetland grandfather → Noel → Gary → Glenn is the book's structural argument.

5. **The Jeremy chapter** is told from Glenn's POV — he wasn't there, Jeremy told him. Epistemic distance is honest and correct. Don't write it from inside the club.

6. **The Needle (Ch 15)** is a dedicated chapter on IV meth use and the progressive nature of addiction. Concrete, not summarised. The specific crossing of that line.

7. **The relapse chapter** (when you're ready): open in the middle of it. No approach from before. Immediate immersion. Explanation is the enemy of this scene.

8. **Gordon Thompson** is Noel's younger brother — co-builder of the empire, not a subordinate. Softer than Noel but still hard. His arc: at some point he expresses distaste for Noel's abuse of Barbara and the children. To be developed.
